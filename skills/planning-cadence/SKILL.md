# Planning Cadence

Grounded in "Planning Cadence" (13 sources across Lenny's Podcast and Newsletter, covering Figma, Shopify, Snowflake, Gong, Notion, Linear, Ramp, Mixpanel).

## When to use
Use this when planning meetings keep producing bruised egos and abandoned plans instead of alignment — usually traceable to nobody having answered who owns what, when, which is exactly the roles-clarity gap the W Framework exists to close. It's also the right tool when a company is running the same planning ritual regardless of stage — a rigid quarterly OKR process built for 500 people will smother a 20-person team still searching for PMF, while ad hoc two-week sprints won't give a 500-person org the coordination it needs for GTM lead time.

## What it does
This skill applies the W Framework's four-step structure (Context from leadership → Plans from teams → Integration by leadership → Buy-in from teams) with explicit role ownership at each step, calibrates planning ceremony to company stage (pre-PMF two-week sprints vs. PMF-stage quarterly cycles vs. multi-product bi-annual OKRs), and applies the 10% planning rule (never spend more than 10% of the execution period actually planning it) as a check against over-processing.

## Method
1. Match planning cadence to company stage first: under 20 people and pre-PMF runs two-week sprints with no formal OKRs ("what are we doing today, this week?"); 100+ people with PMF plans in one-to-two-quarter horizons; 500+ person multi-product orgs run quarterly planning with bi-annual company OKRs.
2. Apply the 10% rule as a sanity check regardless of stage: a one-week sprint gets about half a day of planning, a quarter gets about a week — if planning is eating meaningfully more than that, the strategy underneath is missing, not the process.
3. Run the W Framework's four steps with explicit ownership: Context (leadership shares a draft company strategy — mission, vision, goal, narrative, 3-5 pillars — explicitly as an invitation, not a mandate, naming what's known vs. assumed), Plans (teams respond with proposed strategy, key projects, timeline, resources, risks), Integration (leadership prioritizes, allocates, and concentrates resources on 3-5 bets max — roughly 80% safe short-term, 20% risky long-term), and Buy-in (teams see the integrated plan before it goes wide, get to push back, and expect 5-10% of it to change).
4. Never skip the buy-in step — sharing an integrated plan with team leads first, explaining the trade-offs, and genuinely expecting pushback is what prevents burning through team goodwill; skipping it to save time reliably costs far more time later.
5. Use confidence-tiered rolling roadmaps instead of one fixed annual plan: full detail and ~80% confidence for the current quarter (a real commitment), high-level bullets and ~50% confidence for the next quarter (explicit permission to pivot), and themes only beyond that — resolution should decrease the further out the horizon goes.
6. In genuinely volatile domains, replace rigid OKRs with thematic "seasons": define the season from the actual secular change driving it, set one North Star metric, keep OKRs loose and quarterly, execute in 4-6 week squad goals, and deliberately leave slack for the unplanned.
7. Bridge quarterly/annual plans to daily execution with a lightweight, recurring weekly ritual — a single async question ("what are we doing this week to get closer to our strategic goal?") does more to keep a plan alive than any dashboard review.
8. For growth or experimentation teams specifically, manage capacity as an investment portfolio (iterative experiments / tech-and-UX investments / big bets) with the mix shifted by company stage — roughly a third each at startup stage, shifted toward 70% iterative experiments at growth stage, and 60% big bets once the team is mature and past easy local optimization.

## Inputs
- Current company stage/headcount, to select the right cadence and ceremony level
- The current draft company strategy (or its absence), checked against the five W-Framework ingredients (mission, vision, goal, narrative, pillars)
- How much of the execution period is currently spent planning it, checked against the 10% rule
- The current growth/experimentation portfolio mix (if relevant), checked against the stage-appropriate split

## Output format
A stated planning cadence matched to company stage; a W-Framework role map (what leadership owns vs. what teams own at each of the four steps); a confidence-tiered roadmap view (current quarter high-confidence, next quarter directional, beyond that thematic); and, for growth/experimentation teams, a portfolio mix (experiments/investments/big bets) matched to stage.

## Example
A 60-person company past initial PMF is still running the two-week, no-OKR sprint cadence that worked pre-PMF, and now GTM/sales can't plan campaigns because product direction changes too often to commit to. Applying the stage-match, the team shifts to one-to-two-quarter planning horizons using a lightweight W Framework pass — leadership drafts a short strategy narrative naming 3 pillars, teams propose one-week plans against it, and a simple weekly async check-in keeps the quarter's goal alive day to day without reintroducing heavy ceremony.

## Common pitfalls
- Running the same planning ceremony regardless of company stage — a rigid quarterly OKR process smothers a still-searching pre-PMF team, while ad hoc two-week planning starves a 500-person org of the lead time GTM and sales actually need.
- Skipping the buy-in step to save time, sharing the integrated plan only after it's final — this reliably burns far more team goodwill and trust than the time it saved.
- Planning every future quarter at full resolution instead of using confidence tiers — wasting real effort perfecting a plan for two quarters out that will change anyway before it's executed.
