# Market Mapping

A standalone strategy framework — not sourced from a specific yezi-hub guide. Unlike the other skills in this library, this one isn't grounded in a real Product Fieldwork guide; it's included as a general-purpose consulting methodology worth having on hand.

## When to use
Use this when a decision to enter, expand into, or exit a market needs a real size and shape behind it, and the current view of the market is a single unsourced number in a slide ("this is a $5B market") that nobody can defend under questioning. It's also the right tool when a company is considering an adjacent category and needs to know not just how big it is, but specifically where the under-served, winnable space sits within it. The signal to use it now: someone asks "how do you know it's that big?" and the honest answer is a single analyst report cited without cross-checking. Without rigorous market sizing and mapping, companies either chase markets that are smaller or more contested than believed, or miss real white space because the market was viewed as one undifferentiated block instead of a set of distinct segments with very different supply-demand balances.

## What it does
This skill produces a defensible market size using two independent estimation methods reconciled against each other, then segments demand by underlying need rather than surface category, and overlays where supply is weak relative to demand strength to expose specific, nameable white space. The output is a ranked set of where-to-play options grounded in triangulated numbers, not a single unverified estimate.

## Method
1. Define the market boundary precisely: what customer problem, geography, and buyer type are actually in scope. A market sized too broadly (the whole industry) or too narrowly (only current product form) will produce a misleading number either way.
2. Size the market top-down: start from a broad, credible published figure (industry spend, population x penetration rate, adjacent category size) and narrow it down with a chain of explicit, stated assumptions to reach total addressable market (TAM), serviceable addressable market (SAM), and serviceable obtainable market (SOM).
3. Independently size the market bottom-up: build up from unit economics — number of potential buyers x realistic adoption rate x average spend per buyer — using your own customer, pricing, and pipeline data rather than published figures.
4. Triangulate the two estimates and reconcile the gap explicitly: if top-down and bottom-up land within roughly 20-30% of each other, treat the range as reasonably confident; if they diverge by multiples, identify which specific assumption is driving the gap (usually penetration rate or average spend) and state which method's assumption is more defensible, rather than averaging the two numbers and hiding the disagreement.
5. Segment demand by jobs-to-be-done rather than by demographic or firmographic category alone — the same TAM looks very different once you see it as five distinct buyer needs instead of one undifferentiated pool.
6. For each demand segment, independently assess supply quality: how well current competitors serve that specific need, on price, product fit, and availability. Avoid the trap of assuming supply quality is uniform across the whole market just because a few large incumbents dominate the aggregate share.
7. Plot segments on a 2x2 of demand strength versus supply quality; the high-demand, low-supply-quality quadrant is the white space — attractive, under-served segments where a new entrant has room to win without going head-to-head against a well-served incumbent position.
8. Decompose the market's growth by segment, not just in aggregate, to see whether white space is also where the market is growing (compounding the opportunity) or a shrinking niche (a much weaker opportunity despite looking under-served today).

## Inputs
- Any available published market research or industry sizing reports
- Internal data on current customers, pricing, and win rates to build the bottom-up estimate
- A list of known competitors and a rough sense of their served segments
- The specific geography and buyer scope the sizing should cover

## Output format
A stated market boundary and definition; a triangulated TAM/SAM/SOM with both top-down and bottom-up builds shown and the reconciliation of any gap explained; a demand-strength-vs-supply-quality 2x2 with segments plotted and white space labeled; and a ranked set of 2-3 where-to-play options with the segment-level growth rate for each.

## Example
A company considers entering an adjacent category and sizes it top-down from industry spend data at $1.4B SAM, and bottom-up from realistic adoption of its existing customer base at $1.0B — a gap driven mainly by differing penetration-rate assumptions, reconciled to a working estimate of roughly $1.2B by using the more conservative bottom-up penetration rate. Segmenting demand by job-to-be-done reveals a mid-market segment worth roughly $300M that's growing 22% annually but is currently served only by generic, poorly-fitted incumbent products — the clearest white space on the map. Two beachhead options are ranked, with the mid-market segment recommended first given its combination of under-service and above-market growth.

## Common pitfalls
- Citing a single unsourced or single-method market size without a second, independently built estimate to check it against.
- Treating an entire category as one uniform market instead of segmenting by job-to-be-done, which hides where the real white space and the real competitive intensity actually sit.
- Identifying white space by demand size alone without checking whether that segment is also growing — under-served but shrinking segments are a weak basis for a market entry decision.
