# Running Meetings

Grounded in "Running Effective Meetings — The Meeting OS Guide."

## When to use
Use this when a meeting is being used for all three jobs at once — discovering opinions, discussing disagreement, and deciding — when only the discussion part should ever happen live; discovering opinions live means the loudest or most senior voice anchors everyone else's answer before real debate even starts. It's also the right tool when a meeting habitually ends with no named owner for any action — a meeting without the WHO/WHAT/BY-WHEN test applied at the end is a conversation, not a decision.

## What it does
This skill splits every meeting into discover (async, before — elicit opinions independently so no one anchors on the loudest voice), discuss (the only thing that should happen live, focused specifically on where people disagree), and decide (after, owned, never decided by social pressure in the room). It also gives a hard existence test for whether a recurring meeting should exist at all, a priming checklist to run in the first three minutes, and a closing ritual (the WWW test) that converts talk into named commitments before anyone leaves.

## Method
1. Before defaulting to a live meeting, check whether it should exist at all: is there a real, currently-unsolved problem it addresses, does it have fewer than 10 decision-making attendees and fewer than 3 blocking approvers, and would a more efficient async channel (a Loom, a structured Slack thread, a silent-read document session) do the same job?
2. Move discovery out of the room entirely: before the meeting, collect each person's opinion independently (a point estimate, a written rationale, a forced rank) — never let people see others' answers first, since group talk artificially narrows the true spread of opinion and rewards whoever's most confident, not whoever's most correct.
3. Reserve the live meeting exclusively for discussion — specifically the points of disagreement surfaced by the async discovery step; skip re-litigating the 80% everyone already agrees on.
4. Prime the room in the first three minutes (or in the invite itself): state what category of conversation this is (option generation, tactical problem-solving, efficiency-seeking), which principle applies (speed vs. accuracy), and the desired outcome — skipping this is what causes people optimizing for different things to fight about tactics when the real gap is a misaligned principle.
5. Sell the meeting's purpose before diving into logistics — a brief "why are we here, why now" opener, even two sentences, before stating what's actually needed from the room; people who haven't been re-sold on why this matters today will sit through the whole meeting disengaged.
6. Facilitate toward a decision deliberately: send the agenda and any pre-read a day ahead (cancel if no pre-read went out — the rule enforces itself after the first cancellation), present a small number of real options with one clear recommendation, and edit a shared visual live so decisions get documented as they're made, not reconstructed afterward.
7. Close every meeting with the WWW test on every action item: WHO will do WHAT by WHEN — treat "let's..." and "we should..." as red-flag phrases that need a named owner attached before the meeting ends, not after.
8. Run a blameless post-mortem format for anything that went wrong, distilling feedback into the top 2-3 themes rather than trying to cover everything, and opening with an explicit "we're here to fix the system, not blame people" framing.
9. For a genuinely deadlocked, high-stakes decision that async debate can't resolve, escalate deliberately to a real synchronous session (an offsite, a dedicated block) rather than letting the deadlock drag on indefinitely through repeated inconclusive async rounds.

## Inputs
- The specific recurring meeting being fixed, and its current stated (or unstated) purpose
- Whether discovery is currently happening live (people forming opinions in the room) or has already been moved async
- The typical outcome of the meeting's last few instances — did any produce a named owner and a deadline, or just a conversation?
- Attendee count and blocking-approver count, to check against the existence-test hard limits

## Output format
An existence verdict for the meeting (keep as-is, cut, or replace with an async format); a stated discover/discuss/decide split showing what moved out of the live room; a priming block (category, principle, desired outcome) to use in the invite or opening; and a closing commitment log applying the WWW test to every action item from the most recent instance.

## Example
A weekly "alignment" meeting has 14 attendees and regularly ends with vague next steps. Applying the existence test flags it as over-attended (cut to the 8 people with real decision rights) and reveals most of the time is spent on updates everyone could read async. The fix moves status updates into a written pre-read circulated the day before, reserves the live 30 minutes purely for the 2-3 items with real disagreement, and closes every session by naming a WHO/WHAT/BY-WHEN for each item live, on screen, before anyone leaves.

## Common pitfalls
- Using the live meeting to discover opinions (asking the room what they think) instead of collecting them independently beforehand — this reliably produces anchoring on the loudest or most senior voice rather than real signal.
- Skipping the priming step and diving straight into tactics, so people optimizing for different underlying principles (speed vs. accuracy) end up arguing past each other without realizing the real disagreement is one level up.
- Ending a meeting with vague language ("let's..." "we should...") and no named owner or deadline, so decisions evaporate the moment people leave the room.
