# PM Career Growth

Grounded in "The PM Operating Guide," "Breaking Into AI & Search PM — Ankita Mishra AMA," "Courage as a Service — Vanessa Lee, Shopify," and "The Renaissance Playbook — Mentorship, Networking & Community Craft."

## When to use
Use this when a PM's growth plan is entirely about accumulating more scope or a bigger title, without any concrete artifact (a prototype, a properly-structured strategy doc) proving the judgment a bigger role would actually require. It's also the right tool when someone is preparing for an interview loop and treating "product sense" as something to talk about abstractly, rather than something to demonstrate through a scoped, unsolicited prototype and a document that reflects a real decision hierarchy. It's also the right tool earlier in the funnel — someone deciding how to break into product in the first place, chasing a coveted title over an actual growth trajectory, or avoiding technical rooms out of a felt (not real) skills gap.

## What it does
This skill builds concrete proof of PM judgment through two specific artifacts — an unsolicited prototype scoped to 1-3 hours that demonstrates product instinct, and a document restructured as a decision hierarchy (vision → strategy → roadmap → PRD) rather than independent deliverables — and reframes the PM's core job, at senior levels, as Vanessa Lee's "courage as a service": the willingness to say "we were wrong, we need to change course" backed by real evidence, not authority.

## Method
1. When choosing what to optimize for, pick a high-growth trajectory over a coveted title — a title is a lagging indicator, while joining or staying on the steepest growth curve available is what creates the new, harder problems worth solving, and eventually earns the title anyway.
2. If moving into product for the first time, weigh the entry path deliberately against its risk profile rather than taking whichever door opens first: an internal transition from an adjacent role (marketing, ops, analytics) is slower but arrives with context and trust already built; joining an early-stage startup as its first PM hire is the fastest route to the title, but the experience may not travel if the company doesn't work out; a structured APM/RPM program at a later-stage company is competitive and rigid but comes with real mentorship, a strong peer cohort, and a credential that travels well.
3. If the felt gap is "I don't feel technical enough," treat it as a solvable anxiety, not a permanent ceiling — dogfood the product relentlessly and build rapport with engineers through genuine curiosity about how things work, rather than either avoiding technical rooms or trying to out-credential the people in them.
4. Remember that being credentialed isn't the same as being effective — once everyone in the room is already smart, judgment, curiosity, and follow-through are the real differentiators; a degree or a name-brand background gets you in the door, not up the ladder.
5. Build one small, scoped prototype (1-3 hours, one problem, solved specifically) rather than a strategy doc or slide deck when trying to demonstrate judgment — small and sharp beats ambitious and vague, and tight scoping itself signals seniority.
6. Match the prototype's fidelity and tone to the target company's actual shipping culture — a scrappy company wants scrappy output, a design-obsessed one wants polish, a data-driven one wants the moved metric front and center.
7. Write a one-paragraph framing note alongside any prototype: the problem, what was built, what was deliberately left out and why, and what you'd validate with more information — the "what I cut and why" question is what separates good answers from great ones.
8. Structure every strategy document, PRD, or roadmap as an explicit hierarchy (Vision — 3-5 year destination, rarely mentions features → Strategy — pillars and how the vision gets achieved → Roadmap — which capabilities ship in what order → PRD — one coherent capability) rather than as independent artifacts; if a PRD can't trace back to a strategic pillar, the hierarchy was skipped.
9. Prepare for an interview loop with company-specific research, not generic PM talking points: study the target company's actual competitive position and recent product decisions, and go one layer deeper on anything AI-specific (evals, prompting, recent job-specific questions), since these details change fast enough to need a fresh check per company.
10. If targeting an AI or search-specific PM role, internalize the deterministic-vs-probabilistic mental shift first — traditional products behave identically for every user; AI/search products return a distribution of answers shaped by context — since this reframes what "product sense" even means for that role.
11. At senior levels, reframe the job itself: the PM's highest-leverage act isn't making the call as "CEO of the product," it's injecting courage — saying "we worked on this for three months and I don't think it's right" when that's an unwelcome thing to say, backed by real evidence you've spent time building.
12. Practice the specific technique that makes hard pushback land as collaboration rather than confrontation: be unshakeable on the evidence something is broken (concrete, already-observed examples), but visibly open on the design of the fix (an unpolished sketch, not a finished deck) — this combination is what lets you push hard without cornering the other person.
13. Seek and practice giving/receiving genuine disagreement as a habit, not just at review time — "tell me where I'm wrong" as a standing invitation is what separates a collaborative-in-the-details PM from an ego-driven one who treats being questioned as a threat to their authority.

## Inputs
- The specific skill gap or next-level scope the growth plan is meant to close
- If moving into product for the first time: which entry path is actually available (internal transition, early-stage startup, structured program) and its risk profile
- A target company or role, if preparing for an interview loop specifically
- An existing document (strategy, PRD, roadmap) to check against the decision-hierarchy structure
- A real, evidence-backed disagreement currently being avoided, if the growth area is specifically about exercising more courage

## Output format
A stated growth-trajectory check (is this move chasing a title or a genuinely steeper curve) with, if relevant, an entry-path choice and its risk profile named explicitly; a scoped (1-3 hour) prototype with a one-paragraph framing note (problem, build, cuts, what you'd validate further); a document restructured to trace explicitly from vision through strategy to the specific PRD or roadmap item; a company-specific interview prep brief if targeting a loop; and, where courage is the growth area, a stated piece of unshakeable evidence paired with a deliberately unfinished proposed fix, ready to bring to the relevant stakeholder.

## Example
A generalist with no engineering background wants to break into product and has been putting off applying because "I'm not technical enough." Checked against the growth-not-titles and entry-path lenses: rather than chasing the fastest title (an early-believer startup role with high variance), they take an internal transition from their current ops role, building product influence into the job they already have — slower, but arriving with context and trust already in place — while directly addressing the technical anxiety by dogfooding the product daily and asking engineers genuinely curious questions instead of avoiding their channel. A year later, once actually in the seat, they want to demonstrate readiness for a senior role and, instead of asking for more scope directly, build a 2-hour clickable prototype addressing a specific friction point they'd noticed in the team's own product, with a framing note explaining what they deliberately left out (multi-user support) and why (needs usage data not yet available). Separately, they identify a strategic direction they believe is wrong, spend two weeks building the evidence base (concrete examples of the current approach already failing in production), and bring a rough sketch of an alternative — not a finished deck — to the relevant lead, following the same evidence-firm/design-open pattern that worked in Vanessa Lee's API-versioning story.

## Common pitfalls
- Preparing generic PM interview answers for every company instead of company-specific research — especially skipping the AI-specific layer (evals, prompting depth) for roles that explicitly need it.
- Treating "product sense" as something to describe in the abstract rather than something to demonstrate through a real, scoped prototype with an honest account of what was cut and why.
- Confusing seniority with decision-making authority ("I'm the PM, I make the call") instead of with the willingness to surface hard, evidence-backed disagreement — the actual differentiator at senior levels.
- Chasing the fastest-available title instead of the steepest-available growth trajectory, or picking an entry path into product without weighing its actual risk profile against the alternatives.
- Treating a felt "not technical enough" anxiety as a real, permanent gap instead of a solvable one — dogfooding and genuine engineering curiosity close it faster than avoidance or over-credentialing ever will.
