# PM Career Growth

Grounded in "The PM Operating Guide," "Breaking Into AI & Search PM — Ankita Mishra AMA," and "Courage as a Service — Vanessa Lee, Shopify."

## When to use
Use this when a PM's growth plan is entirely about accumulating more scope or a bigger title, without any concrete artifact (a prototype, a properly-structured strategy doc) proving the judgment a bigger role would actually require. It's also the right tool when someone is preparing for an interview loop and treating "product sense" as something to talk about abstractly, rather than something to demonstrate through a scoped, unsolicited prototype and a document that reflects a real decision hierarchy.

## What it does
This skill builds concrete proof of PM judgment through two specific artifacts — an unsolicited prototype scoped to 1-3 hours that demonstrates product instinct, and a document restructured as a decision hierarchy (vision → strategy → roadmap → PRD) rather than independent deliverables — and reframes the PM's core job, at senior levels, as Vanessa Lee's "courage as a service": the willingness to say "we were wrong, we need to change course" backed by real evidence, not authority.

## Method
1. Build one small, scoped prototype (1-3 hours, one problem, solved specifically) rather than a strategy doc or slide deck when trying to demonstrate judgment — small and sharp beats ambitious and vague, and tight scoping itself signals seniority.
2. Match the prototype's fidelity and tone to the target company's actual shipping culture — a scrappy company wants scrappy output, a design-obsessed one wants polish, a data-driven one wants the moved metric front and center.
3. Write a one-paragraph framing note alongside any prototype: the problem, what was built, what was deliberately left out and why, and what you'd validate with more information — the "what I cut and why" question is what separates good answers from great ones.
4. Structure every strategy document, PRD, or roadmap as an explicit hierarchy (Vision — 3-5 year destination, rarely mentions features → Strategy — pillars and how the vision gets achieved → Roadmap — which capabilities ship in what order → PRD — one coherent capability) rather than as independent artifacts; if a PRD can't trace back to a strategic pillar, the hierarchy was skipped.
5. Prepare for an interview loop with company-specific research, not generic PM talking points: study the target company's actual competitive position and recent product decisions, and go one layer deeper on anything AI-specific (evals, prompting, recent job-specific questions), since these details change fast enough to need a fresh check per company.
6. If targeting an AI or search-specific PM role, internalize the deterministic-vs-probabilistic mental shift first — traditional products behave identically for every user; AI/search products return a distribution of answers shaped by context — since this reframes what "product sense" even means for that role.
7. At senior levels, reframe the job itself: the PM's highest-leverage act isn't making the call as "CEO of the product," it's injecting courage — saying "we worked on this for three months and I don't think it's right" when that's an unwelcome thing to say, backed by real evidence you've spent time building.
8. Practice the specific technique that makes hard pushback land as collaboration rather than confrontation: be unshakeable on the evidence something is broken (concrete, already-observed examples), but visibly open on the design of the fix (an unpolished sketch, not a finished deck) — this combination is what lets you push hard without cornering the other person.
9. Seek and practice giving/receiving genuine disagreement as a habit, not just at review time — "tell me where I'm wrong" as a standing invitation is what separates a collaborative-in-the-details PM from an ego-driven one who treats being questioned as a threat to their authority.

## Inputs
- The specific skill gap or next-level scope the growth plan is meant to close
- A target company or role, if preparing for an interview loop specifically
- An existing document (strategy, PRD, roadmap) to check against the decision-hierarchy structure
- A real, evidence-backed disagreement currently being avoided, if the growth area is specifically about exercising more courage

## Output format
A scoped (1-3 hour) prototype with a one-paragraph framing note (problem, build, cuts, what you'd validate further); a document restructured to trace explicitly from vision through strategy to the specific PRD or roadmap item; a company-specific interview prep brief if targeting a loop; and, where courage is the growth area, a stated piece of unshakeable evidence paired with a deliberately unfinished proposed fix, ready to bring to the relevant stakeholder.

## Example
A mid-level PM wants to demonstrate readiness for a senior role and, instead of asking for more scope directly, builds a 2-hour clickable prototype addressing a specific friction point they'd noticed in the team's own product, with a framing note explaining what they deliberately left out (multi-user support) and why (needs usage data not yet available). Separately, they identify a strategic direction they believe is wrong, spend two weeks building the evidence base (concrete examples of the current approach already failing in production), and bring a rough sketch of an alternative — not a finished deck — to the relevant lead, following the same evidence-firm/design-open pattern that worked in Vanessa Lee's API-versioning story.

## Common pitfalls
- Preparing generic PM interview answers for every company instead of company-specific research — especially skipping the AI-specific layer (evals, prompting depth) for roles that explicitly need it.
- Treating "product sense" as something to describe in the abstract rather than something to demonstrate through a real, scoped prototype with an honest account of what was cut and why.
- Confusing seniority with decision-making authority ("I'm the PM, I make the call") instead of with the willingness to surface hard, evidence-backed disagreement — the actual differentiator at senior levels.
