# Enterprise Sales Motion

Grounded in "Go-to-Market, Pricing & Sales — The Field Guide" and "Enterprise Analytics & PM Playbook."

## When to use
Use this when an enterprise deal is being pitched on "art of the possible" upside messaging, when roughly 80% of enterprise buyers are actually buying to reduce risk, not chase upside — a mismatch that stalls deals late in the cycle. It's also the right tool when a PM needs to move an initiative through engineering, sales, or leadership without formal authority over any of them — enterprise motions run on cross-functional influence more than direct reporting lines. The signal to use it now: a deal stalls and the account team can't say specifically why, beyond "price" — often a proxy for never having reached or convinced the real economic buyer.

## What it does
This skill structures the enterprise motion around what actually converts enterprise buyers: risk reduction framing over upside framing, a pricing/gating structure that reserves premium features (SSO, audit logs, compliance reports) behind "contact sales," and the value equation for pricing large deals defensibly. It layers in the influence tactics enterprise PMs need without direct authority — data-backed priority stacks for engineers, adoption dashboards, and stakeholder-specific levers for designers, senior managers, and customers.

## Method
1. Frame the pitch around risk reduction, not upside — roughly 80% of enterprise buyers are avoiding the pain of missing a target or being outdone, not chasing "art of the possible" vision; adopting a new vendor is a career bet for the buyer, and much of their decision is "I don't want to screw this up."
2. Replace a generic discovery interrogation with a collaborative whiteboard of the customer's actual architecture — they leave with something useful, and the seller learns their whole stack in the process.
3. Add value at every touchpoint even when a prospect doesn't buy now (a benchmark of their performance vs. peers, for instance) — enterprise buyers you "lose" often return years later, and the touchpoint is what they remember.
4. Structure pricing and packaging for enterprise specifically: offer one or two cheaper self-serve plans with basic functionality, and gate what enterprises can't live without (SSO, audit logs, compliance reports, data residency) behind "contact sales" — often at a materially higher price point, since enterprise value varies too much per customer for one fixed number.
5. Price large deals with the value equation: sit with the internal champion, quantify the value the tool will deliver in dollar terms, and price at roughly a third of that value — the champion needs this number to justify the purchase to their own CFO.
6. Match pricing structure to what can actually fund a sales motion: the 5:1 rule (new signed ARR should be roughly 5x a salesperson's total comp) determines whether the business supports account executives at all, or needs a self-serve/inside-sales model instead.
7. Influence engineers with data, not asks: instead of "please build this, it's important," show a data-backed priority stack ("Feature A moves the target metric 10pp, Feature B moves it 2pp") — engineers respond to evidence of impact, not urgency framing.
8. Match the influence lever to each stakeholder type: engineers want impact data and visible adoption dashboards (a weekly "Friday feature review" builds ownership); designers want to see how a piece completes the end-to-end workflow they care about; senior managers want feature work translated into revenue/engagement impact in a 1-2 page weekly summary tied to their own OKRs.

## Inputs
- The specific enterprise deal or motion being built, and where it's currently stalling
- The buyer's actual economic decision-maker and what they need to justify the purchase internally (the "champion" question)
- Current pricing/packaging structure, to check whether enterprise-required features are properly gated
- For internal influence: which stakeholder (engineering, design, leadership) needs to be moved, and what data is available on impact

## Output format
A stated risk-reduction framing for the pitch (not an upside pitch); a pricing/gating structure separating self-serve tiers from "contact sales" enterprise features; a value-equation-based price point with the underlying dollar calculation shown; a 5:1-rule check on whether the deal size supports an AE-based sales motion; and, for internal influence, a data-backed priority stack or weekly dashboard tailored to the specific stakeholder being moved.

## Example
An enterprise deal stalls after several promising calls, blamed internally on "price." Applying the risk-reduction lens surfaces the real issue: the pitch had been framed entirely around upside ("here's what you could achieve"), never around the buyer's actual anxiety (a failed vendor evaluation on their record). Reframing around de-risking — a specific benchmark showing peers already using the tool, plus a phased pilot with a clear rollback plan — reopens the conversation with the actual economic buyer, who until then had never been directly engaged. The final price is set using the value equation (a third of quantified time-savings value) rather than matched to a competitor's list price.

## Common pitfalls
- Pitching enterprise buyers on upside and vision when roughly 80% are actually motivated by risk reduction — a mismatched frame stalls a deal that a de-risking pitch would have closed.
- Publishing one fixed enterprise price on a website, leaving money on the table for high-value accounts and pricing out smaller enterprise buyers who'd convert at a lower number — "contact sales" gating exists precisely to price-discriminate fairly.
- Asking engineers for roadmap priority with urgency alone ("this is really important") instead of a data-backed comparison showing relative impact — engineers respond to evidence, not appeals.
