# Pricing Strategy

Grounded in "Monetization, Tokens & Strategy," "Monetization Experiment Guideline — Seat vs. Value Pricing," and "Go-to-Market, Pricing & Sales — The Field Guide," plus a standalone value-vs-structure-vs-discipline pricing-diagnosis framework (not sourced from a specific yezi-hub guide) that applies beyond AI-specific pricing.

## When to use
Use this when discounting has become routine, win rates are healthy but margins are eroding, or the organization genuinely doesn't know whether its prices are too high, too low, or simply structured wrong for how customers actually derive value — the signal is sales reps having unofficial, well-known discount levels that exceed the official policy, meaning list price has stopped functioning as a real anchor. It's also the right tool ahead of a new product or tier launch, when price is often set by copying a competitor or by gut feel rather than by any estimate of value created. For an AI feature specifically, use it when pricing defaults to seat-based logic without checking whether the feature's actual autonomy and attribution level would support real outcome-based pricing instead — the highest-value quadrant (high autonomy, high attribution) can capture 25-50% of value created versus 10-20% for classic seat-based SaaS. And use it whenever a pricing change is about to ship broadly with no baseline, no guardrail, and no rollback plan.

## What it does
This skill first separates a pricing problem into its actual root cause — a value-communication gap, a structural mismatch between price metric and value delivered, or a discounting-discipline failure — using willingness-to-pay evidence rather than opinion, and models the price-volume trade-off to find the margin-maximizing zone. For AI products specifically, it also picks the right pricing archetype from the autonomy/attribution 2×2 (seat-based, hybrid, usage-based, or outcome-based) matched to what the product can actually prove, and reframes any pilot or PoC as a co-created business case rather than a technical validation. It closes by converting the diagnosis into a concrete, sequenced action plan — covering structure, price fences, and discount governance — and applies the seat-vs-usage experimentation discipline (baseline, narrow hypothesis, predeclared guardrails, contained rollout) to any pricing change before it ships broadly.

## Method
1. Anchor to value first: estimate the economic value the offer creates for each customer segment (cost saved, revenue generated, risk reduced) relative to the next-best alternative, including doing nothing. This value estimate — not cost-plus or competitor matching — is the ceiling that should inform the pricing conversation.
2. Map current price realization against that value estimate by segment: where realized price (list price minus average discount) sits far below estimated value, that's a value-capture gap; where it sits above, that's a retention and churn risk waiting to surface.
3. Probe willingness to pay directly where data allows, using a structured price-sensitivity survey approach that identifies the range between "too cheap to be credible" and "too expensive to consider," triangulated against actual win/loss data at different discount levels — stated willingness to pay is directionally useful but should never be taken as precise without behavioral confirmation.
4. Diagnose the structural fit of the pricing metric itself: does the company charge for the unit that actually tracks with the value customers receive (per seat, per transaction, per outcome), or has the metric drifted out of alignment as the product or customer base evolved? Audit discounting behavior separately — pull the distribution of actual realized discounts by deal size, segment, and rep, and identify where the "unofficial" discount floor has drifted from official policy — this frequently reveals the real problem is discipline, not the price itself. Design price fences where willingness to pay clearly differs by segment (self-serve vs. enterprise, month-to-month vs. annual) so value-based differentiation is structurally enforced rather than left to ad hoc negotiation.
5. For an AI product or feature, place it on the autonomy × attribution 2×2 honestly: low autonomy/low attribution fits seat-based pricing; low autonomy with high attribution (a copilot with provable impact) fits hybrid seat-plus-consumption; high autonomy/low attribution fits pure usage-based; high autonomy with high attribution is the only quadrant where genuine outcome-based pricing works — don't jump to outcome-based before attribution can actually be proven. An incumbent already on seats should take the honest incremental path first (layer token/credit provisions onto existing tiers so running out creates a natural upgrade conversation) before attempting a full outcome-priced bolt-on product (the Intercom Fin pattern — priced per AI-resolved ticket, $0 if a human intervenes).
6. Reframe any AI pilot or proof-of-concept as a co-created business case, not a technical validation: charge for it (to filter serious buyers from tire-kickers), never let the fee anchor the eventual commercial price, and co-build the ROI model with the customer's own inputs so they can't challenge the output later since they agreed to the inputs. Negotiate with give-and-get discipline: never concede a discount for free, and present options (a flat price vs. a lower-flat-plus-percentage-of-value structure) rather than a single number.
7. Translate the diagnosis into a sequenced action plan: which segment gets repriced first, what new structure or fences are introduced, what the new discount floor and approval process are, and how the change will be communicated to sales and existing customers to manage the transition.
8. Before running any pricing experiment, establish the baseline (current conversion, ARPU, retention, support volume by segment) as the control every result gets judged against. Write one narrow hypothesis covering exactly one pricing variable and one segment, predeclare guardrails (acceptable drops in conversion/retention, margin limits) and a rollback plan before launch, and roll out to a contained cohort only (new signups, one region) — never existing accounts first.
9. Judge the result by the whole metric shape, not one headline number: a pricing change that lifts ARPU while quietly damaging activation, retention, or spiking support tickets is a failure dressed as a win. For anything token/compute-priced, remember token cost is a dial, not a fixed fact — tune it via caching rather than passing raw, unoptimized compute cost straight through to the customer.

## Inputs
- Current price list, actual realized prices, and discount data by segment, deal size, and rep
- Cost-to-serve by segment or product line, and win/loss data with discount level at which each deal closed or was lost
- Any existing willingness-to-pay research, or willingness to run a lightweight pricing survey
- For AI/token-priced products: the product's actual autonomy level and attribution capability, current cache hit rate, and input:output token ratio
- Baseline metrics (conversion, ARPU, retention, support volume) by segment, before any pricing test

## Output format
A value-versus-realized-price map by segment; a willingness-to-pay range with behavioral triangulation against win/loss data; a discount-distribution audit flagging where actual practice has drifted from policy; for AI products, a stated position on the autonomy/attribution 2×2 with the resulting recommended archetype and, for any pilot, a co-created ROI model priced with a range or percentage-of-value framing; a sequenced pricing action plan covering structure changes, price fences, and discount governance; and, for any pricing change, a narrow one-variable hypothesis with predeclared guardrails, a contained-cohort rollout plan, and a whole-metric-shape judgment criterion.

## Example
A B2B software company sees healthy win rates but declining margin and discovers average discretionary discounts have crept from an official 10% ceiling to a realized average of 27% in the enterprise segment; the value analysis shows enterprise customers are receiving roughly 4x the offer's price in quantifiable value, meaning the problem is discount discipline, not value perception. Separately, an AI customer-support product currently priced per seat is considering an outcome-based ticket-resolution add-on: mapped onto the 2×2, the core seat-based product stays where it is, but the new autonomous resolution feature is scoped as its own bolt-on priced strictly per AI-resolved ticket, and a 30-day pilot is framed as building the ROI model together with the customer's own support data rather than as a free technical trial.

## Common pitfalls
- Treating a discounting problem as a pricing-level problem and cutting list price, which does nothing to fix an undisciplined approval process and simply resets a lower floor for the same behavior to recur at.
- Relying only on stated willingness-to-pay survey data without triangulating it against actual behavioral win/loss evidence, or setting one price for an entire customer base when willingness to pay clearly diverges by segment.
- Reaching for outcome-based AI pricing before attribution can actually be proven with data, or presenting a pilot as a free technical validation instead of a co-created, filtered business case.
- Shipping a pricing change to the full existing customer base at once instead of a contained new-signup cohort with predeclared guardrails, making it impossible to cleanly attribute the resulting metric shifts to the change itself.
