# Breaking Into Product

Grounded in "Breaking Into AI & Search PM — Ankita Mishra AMA" (Senior PM, Atlassian Rovo search & chat; previously Microsoft Search).

## When to use
Use this when someone from an adjacent discipline (engineering, data, support, ops) wants a PM role and doesn't know what actually differs about a role with "AI" or "search" in the title versus a traditional PM job they might already be qualified for. It's also the right tool right before an interview loop, when generic PM interview prep ("tell me about a time...") isn't enough because the role will also probe AI-specific judgment. The signal to use it now: someone is applying with the same prep to every company regardless of what that specific company's AI maturity or role flavor actually is.

## What it does
This skill first sorts which of the three AI PM flavors (AI Experience, AI Builder/Technical, or AI-Enhanced) a target role actually is, so prep effort goes to the right skill bucket. It then builds real proof of work without requiring an existing AI PM title, and applies a company-specific interview prep method (the "Virat Kohli" method — scout the opponent, scout the field, keep fundamentals sharp, go one layer deeper on AI specifics) rather than one generic prep script for every interview.

## Method
1. Identify which of the three AI PM flavors the target role actually is: AI Experience PM (the user-facing behavior of an AI product — search intent detection, an assistant's responses), AI Builder/Technical PM (the models and infrastructure themselves — cost, latency, architecture), or AI-Enhanced PM (a non-AI product, but using AI tools daily to move faster). Each demands different prep.
2. Separate the two skill buckets explicitly: Bucket 1 (universal PM — customer empathy, product framing, PRDs, stakeholder alignment) needs no re-learning; Bucket 2 (AI-specific — evals, edge-case discovery, token/latency/cost tradeoffs, treating data as a product asset, running graduated experiments) is the actual gap to close.
3. Internalize the core mental shift: traditional/deterministic products behave the same way for every user every time; AI/probabilistic products return a distribution of possible answers shaped by context and personalization — specs describe a probability of answers, not one fixed answer.
4. Build proof of work without an existing AI PM title: follow people actually doing the job, exhaust free courses before paying for anything, build one small AI-assisted mini-project to speak with real specifics instead of buzzwords, look for an internal transfer first if your company already has an AI team, and find one real mentor (expect to ask several people before one says yes).
5. Before each specific interview, run the four-part prep: study the opponent (the company's competitive AI position and what its own job posting emphasizes), study the field (Amazon leans on leadership principles, Google goes deep on the specific product — tailor accordingly), keep universal PM fundamentals sharp without re-revising them, and go one layer deeper on AI specifics (prompting, evals, recent job-specific questions) since these change fast enough to warrant a fresh check per company.
6. Prepare the one recurring question in real depth: "walk me through one project 0-to-1, discovery through execution to launch" — with specifics on what you built, how you used AI, and what you'd do differently.
7. Never claim a tool you haven't actually used — interviewers working in AI can tell the difference fast with one follow-up question, and fabrication is far worse than admitted unfamiliarity.
8. Once in the role, expect the cadence itself to change: AI/search products get checked daily (sometimes hourly) via top and failed queries, not reviewed at a sprint boundary — build that expectation into how you describe your own working style in interviews.

## Inputs
- The specific target role's job posting and company, to identify the AI PM flavor and tailor prep
- What proof of work already exists (side projects, internal exposure, relevant transferable experience)
- Any AI tools already used hands-on (be ready to speak to them with real specifics)
- Whether an internal transfer path exists at the person's current company

## Output format
A named AI PM flavor (Experience / Builder / Enhanced) for the target role; a gap analysis between Bucket 1 (already have) and Bucket 2 (needs building) skills; a company-specific interview prep brief following the four-part method; and a rehearsed answer to the "walk me through one project 0-to-1" question with real specifics.

## Example
An engineer wants to move into a search-PM role at a company whose job posting emphasizes "query understanding" and "relevance." The flavor is identified as AI Experience PM. Bucket 2 prep focuses on relevance/latency/hallucination-rate metrics and the experimentation ladder (spec → small test cohort → wider cohort → private preview → public preview) rather than model architecture. A one-week mini-project — a small AI-assisted prototype solving a real query-understanding problem — becomes the concrete answer to the 0-to-1 question, replacing buzzword-only claims about "AI experience."

## Common pitfalls
- Preparing the same generic AI-PM answers for every company instead of scouting each company's specific AI maturity and role flavor, the way an athlete would actually scout a specific opponent.
- Claiming familiarity with a tool never actually used — a fast follow-up question in an AI-fluent interview will expose it, and fabrication reads far worse than an honest "I haven't used that one yet."
- Treating "Bucket 1" fundamentals (stakeholder alignment, product framing) as needing a total overhaul for AI roles, when the actual gap is almost always Bucket 2 (evals, cost/latency tradeoffs, data-as-asset thinking).
