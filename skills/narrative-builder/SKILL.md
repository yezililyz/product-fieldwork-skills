# Narrative Builder

A standalone strategy framework — not sourced from a specific yezi-hub guide. Unlike the other skills in this library, this one isn't grounded in a real Product Fieldwork guide; it's included as a general-purpose consulting methodology worth having on hand.

## When to use
Use this when solid analysis exists but the way it's currently written or presented buries the point — a reader has to get through several pages or slides before understanding what's actually being recommended and why. It's especially important ahead of a high-stakes review where the audience is time-constrained, skeptical, or actively looking for a reason to poke holes in the recommendation. The signal to use it now: a colleague reads the draft and asks "wait, what are we actually recommending?" after the second page. Without a disciplined narrative structure, even excellent analysis fails to persuade — the reader disengages before reaching the insight, or the recommendation survives the first read but collapses under the first hard question in the room because nobody rehearsed the pushback in advance.

## What it does
This skill restructures a recommendation into an answer-first narrative built around one governing thought supported by a small number of independently sufficient arguments, each backed by a specific piece of evidence, and then produces that same story at multiple lengths so it lands whether the audience has 30 seconds or 60 minutes. It closes by pre-building the answers to the hardest questions the audience is likely to ask, so the presenter is never caught improvising a defense in the room.

## Method
1. Distill the entire recommendation into a single governing thought — one sentence stating the recommendation and its primary justification, specific enough that someone could act on it if that sentence were the only thing they heard.
2. Structure the supporting logic as a small set (typically three) of independent arguments that each, on their own, would be sufficient reason to accept the governing thought — not a chain of dependent steps where the argument collapses if one link is challenged.
3. Attach one piece of concrete evidence or exhibit under each supporting argument — a chart, a data point, a comparison — chosen because it's the single strongest proof of that argument, not because it was already in the deck.
4. Frame the opening using a situation-complication-question-answer structure: state the shared situation, name the complication that changed things, pose the question that follows from it, and only then reveal the answer — this is what makes the "why now" impossible to ignore rather than an afterthought.
5. Build a Day-1 summary (a single slide or paragraph capturing the whole narrative) and then produce the story at four lengths — a 30-second version for a hallway conversation, a 2-minute version for an elevator pitch, a 10-minute version for a working session, and a 60-minute version with full supporting detail — each a genuine compression of the same governing thought and arguments, not a truncated version that cuts the ending.
6. Pressure-test the argument structure for the common failure mode of building a narrative that only works if the audience accepts every step in sequence; if any single supporting argument, removed, would cause the whole recommendation to collapse, restructure so the arguments are genuinely independent and mutually reinforcing instead.
7. Identify the hostile questions: the specific objections a skeptical, well-informed member of the audience would raise, drawing on known organizational politics, prior failed initiatives, and the weakest parts of the evidence base.
8. Write the answer to each hostile question in advance, in the same crisp, evidence-backed style as the main narrative, so the presenter can answer immediately and specifically rather than being visibly caught off guard.

## Inputs
- The underlying recommendation and the full body of analysis and evidence behind it
- Knowledge of the audience: their likely objections, prior positions, and what has failed before in this organization
- The context and stakes of the meeting or document where the narrative will be used
- Any exhibits, charts, or data already produced that could serve as evidence under each argument

## Output format
A one-sentence governing thought; three independent supporting arguments each with one named exhibit; the narrative written at four lengths (30 seconds, 2 minutes, 10 minutes, 60 minutes); and a hostile-question prep sheet listing each anticipated objection with its prepared answer.

## Example
A cost-reduction recommendation is restructured around the governing thought "cut $12M from procurement by consolidating three vendor categories, with no service-level impact." The three supporting arguments — vendor overlap is costing 15% in redundant contracts, two categories have viable single-source alternatives already vetted, and the consolidation timeline avoids disrupting the upcoming product launch — each carry one chart. The hostile-question sheet pre-answers the sharpest anticipated pushback: "what happens if the single-source vendor fails to deliver," with a specific contingency already built into the plan rather than left for the room to surface.

## Common pitfalls
- Building a chain of dependent arguments where challenging one early point unravels the whole recommendation, instead of independent arguments that each stand on their own.
- Leading with background and analysis and saving the recommendation for the end, losing a time-constrained audience before the point is made.
- Walking into a high-stakes review without pre-drafting answers to the hardest likely questions, forcing real-time improvisation on the most contested points.
