# Team Culture

Grounded in "Courage as a Service — Vanessa Lee, Shopify" and "The Monsters and the Gym — A Total Quality Story."

## When to use
Use this when a culture problem is being addressed with a values poster or a slide deck instead of something that actually lands — a story (the customer-as-"monster" parable, or a real story of reversing a CEO's decision in one meeting) changes behavior in a way an abstract framework rarely does, because stories acknowledge that every team and situation is genuinely different while frameworks offer false assurance that following steps guarantees an outcome. It's also the right tool when "ego-driven" leadership behavior (treating being questioned as a threat, optimizing for looking decisive over being right) is quietly discouraging the team from surfacing real disagreement.

## What it does
This skill applies the Total Quality parable's model of continuous, distributed daily improvement (train the whole team's brain and heart, not just leadership's; look for hundreds of small daily gains instead of one big annual push) alongside Vanessa Lee's reframing of what a senior team member's real job is — injecting courage, not asserting authority — and gives concrete diagnostic questions and rituals for building both into a team's actual daily behavior.

## Method
1. Reframe the customer relationship the way the parable does: the customer is demanding, quietly leaves rather than complains, and tells roughly 30 other people about a bad experience but rarely tells you directly — design feedback loops that actively go looking for silent dissatisfaction rather than waiting for complaints that mostly never arrive.
2. Shift from management-by-control to management-by-education explicitly: instead of correcting what people do wrong, reinforce what they do well, and trust people to develop judgment rather than waiting to be told exactly what to do — this is the specific mechanism that activates real ownership rather than compliance.
3. Institute a genuinely daily (not annual) improvement habit: before starting work, try to improve even slightly on the very last thing you did yesterday — continuous improvement compounds from many small distributed gains, not one big top-down push.
4. Make "tell me where I'm wrong" a standing, repeated invitation rather than a one-off — this is what separates a collaborative-in-the-details leader (who cares about specifics precisely because details compound) from an ego-driven one who treats being questioned as a threat to authority.
5. Model courage as evidence-backed pushback, not authority: reward the team member who says "we worked on this for three months and I don't think it's right," provided it's backed by real evidence — this is the single highest-leverage cultural behavior a senior team member can demonstrate and reward in others.
6. Use the unfinished-artifact tactic as a cultural norm, not just an individual tactic: bring rough, visibly-unfinished proposals into disagreements rather than fully-polished ones — a finished presentation signals "I've already done all the thinking," which discourages the very contribution a healthy culture wants to invite.
7. Reinforce that being deep in the details (caring about a specific convention, a specific edge case) is not the same as micromanaging — the difference is whether questioning invites collaboration or shuts it down; make this distinction explicit so senior involvement doesn't get mistaken for control.
8. Use a real story rather than an abstract framework when a culture point needs to actually land — frameworks offer false assurance ("follow these steps and you'll land in the right place") that breaks the moment the team or situation doesn't match the original case; a concrete story acknowledges the messiness and sticks.

## Inputs
- The specific culture behavior being reinforced or discouraged (courage/pushback, ownership, customer devotion)
- Any recent example of the team either surfacing or suppressing a hard disagreement, to use as a concrete teaching moment
- Current feedback-loop design — does it wait for complaints, or actively look for the silent, "quietly leaving" kind of dissatisfaction the parable describes?
- Whether senior involvement in details is currently landing as collaboration or as control, from the team's perspective

## Output format
A stated cultural behavior to reinforce (e.g., evidence-backed pushback, daily small-improvement habit, active dissatisfaction-seeking) with a concrete story or example illustrating it rather than an abstract principle; a specific ritual (a standing "tell me where I'm wrong" invitation, a daily improvement habit, an active customer-listening mechanism) the team can actually adopt; and an explicit distinction between senior-detail-involvement-as-collaboration and senior-detail-involvement-as-control, stated so the team can tell which one is happening.

## Example
A team's retros consistently surface no real disagreement, and leadership suspects people are afraid to push back. Instead of a generic "psychological safety" slide, the team is told the specific story of a junior PM who reversed a CEO's stated position in one 45-minute meeting using unshakeable evidence paired with a deliberately rough sketch of the fix — and the team adopts the same pattern explicitly: bring the evidence first, keep the proposed fix visibly unfinished, and expect genuine pushback on the "how" rather than treating disagreement as a threat to the plan.

## Common pitfalls
- Trying to fix a culture problem with a values statement or slide deck instead of a concrete, specific story the team can actually recognize themselves in and repeat.
- Treating "we're deep in the details here" as inherently healthy without checking whether it's actually landing as micromanagement — the same behavior reads completely differently depending on whether questions invite collaboration or shut it down.
- Waiting for customers (or team members) to complain before treating something as a problem — the parable's central warning is that both mostly leave quietly rather than complain directly, so a culture that only reacts to loud complaints is missing most of the real signal.
