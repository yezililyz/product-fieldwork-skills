# Referrals & Word of Mouth

Grounded in "Acquisition Channel Strategy — The Distribution Engine Guide," plus the referral-cost math from "Beyond Prioritization — The Field Guide."

## When to use
Use this when a referral program is being greenlit purely on reach math (more signups, wider funnel) without checking the real cost side — a referral incentive doubles cost of acquisition by rewarding both referrer and referred, invites fraud and fake accounts, and often has no attached revenue model, making it look great on a simple reach estimate while being a leaky bucket in practice. It's also the right tool when word-of-mouth is assumed to just happen rather than being engineered — genuine organic virality is one of seven distinct, identifiable distribution advantages, not a vague hope.

## What it does
This skill checks whether a referral or word-of-mouth motion is actually one of the company's real structural advantages (rather than a generic tactic bolted on hopefully), applies the honest full-cost math that catches the referral-program mirage before launch, and designs the actual mechanics — a viral loop, a remarkable story, or founder-led hustle — that produce genuine word-of-mouth rather than assuming it will emerge on its own.

## Method
1. Check whether a unique viral loop is a real structural advantage before designing a referral program around it — a viral loop has to be architected into the product from day one (supply-driving-demand, bottom-up team adoption); it's very difficult to bolt on to a product that wasn't built for it.
2. Run the full cost math on any referral incentive before shipping it — the check that exposes "the referral-program mirage": does it roughly double cost of acquisition (rewarding both sides), what's the realistic fraud/fake-account exposure, and is there an actual revenue model attached — or does it just look great on a reach estimate (or a RICE score) while quietly eroding margin, driving returns and support tickets, and inviting abuse?
3. If pursuing organic word-of-mouth instead of an incentivized program, check for a genuinely remarkable story worth talking about — the bar is higher than most teams assume; "we built a useful product" isn't remarkable, but a real, specific, hard-won achievement or an unusually clever moment often is.
4. Consider founder-led hustle as the honest fallback advantage when none of the other six distribution advantages apply — sheer effort (direct outreach, in-person demos, manual concierge onboarding) genuinely works for early word-of-mouth even without a structural edge.
5. Distinguish word-of-mouth as a "turbo boost" (a one-off attention spike — a viral video, a stunt, a co-marketing moment) from word-of-mouth as a genuine engine (a sustained viral loop) — turbo boosts are real and valuable but unrepeatable; don't mistake one for the other when planning a channel strategy.
6. If building a referral mechanic, design it as a loop with a real incentive on both sides that doesn't compound acquisition cost unsustainably — model the actual unit economics (referrer reward + referred reward + expected fraud rate) against the value of the referred customer before committing to the incentive structure.
7. Track referral/word-of-mouth performance the same way any acquisition channel should be measured — payback period, not just raw signups — since a referral channel that looks cheap on cost-per-signup can still have a terrible payback period once both-sided rewards and fraud losses are included.
8. Treat organic word-of-mouth as something to instrument and observe, not just hope for — track where unprompted mentions and referrals are already happening organically before building a formal incentive program on top of a signal that might not actually be there yet.

## Inputs
- The current or proposed referral incentive structure, with both-sided reward costs stated explicitly
- Any existing signal of organic word-of-mouth already happening, before formalizing an incentive program
- Whether the product has an architecturally real viral loop (supply drives demand) or would need one bolted on
- The realistic fraud/abuse exposure for any proposed referral reward structure

## Output format
A stated verdict on whether a viral loop is a real structural advantage or something being force-fit onto the product; a full cost model for any referral incentive (both-sided reward cost, fraud exposure, attached revenue model) compared honestly against raw reach; and, where relevant, a distinction between a one-off word-of-mouth turbo boost being planned and a genuine repeatable engine.

## Example
A team proposes a referral program projecting strong signup growth based on reach alone. Running the full cost math reveals the double-sided reward would roughly double blended CAC, with no clear revenue model attached to the referred segment specifically — the "referral-program mirage." Instead, the team finds real organic word-of-mouth already happening in a specific power-user community and redirects effort toward supporting that channel (better sharing tools, a lightweight ambassador program) rather than launching the broader incentivized referral program that looked appealing on a simple reach projection.

## Common pitfalls
- Greenlighting a referral incentive based on reach math alone, missing that it roughly doubles acquisition cost, invites fraud, and often has no revenue model attached — the referral-program mirage.
- Assuming word-of-mouth will emerge organically without checking whether the product actually has one of the seven real distribution advantages (a genuine viral loop, a remarkable story) that would produce it.
- Confusing a one-off attention spike (a viral moment, a stunt) with a repeatable, engineered word-of-mouth engine — treating an unrepeatable turbo boost as if it were a sustainable channel.
