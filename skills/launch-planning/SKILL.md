# Launch Planning

Grounded in "Modern GTM Playbook • Jean Grosser" and "Go-to-Market, Pricing & Sales — The Field Guide."

## When to use
Use this when launch planning treats GTM as marketing-plus-sales handled after the product is built, rather than as an integrated system (marketing, sales, CS, support, partnerships — every function that touches a customer or makes a dollar) designed alongside the product. It's also the right tool when segmentation stops at "SMB / mid-market / enterprise," missing the axes (growth potential, workload/traffic, promote signal) that actually predict revenue — a small-headcount, high-traffic startup can belong in enterprise treatment despite looking like SMB on paper. The signal to use it now: the launch plan has a pricing page and a sales deck but no defined moment where GTM engineering or channel sequencing was actually designed.

## What it does
This skill plans launch GTM using the PACE sequence (pain → audience → channel → expansion) for who to reach and how, layers in Jean Grosser's "GTM as a product" philosophy (map the entire buying journey, design every touchpoint deliberately, use richer segmentation axes than firmographics alone), and makes the PLG-vs-sales timing decision early and deliberately rather than reactively once growth stalls.

## Method
1. Establish the pain before anything else, per PACE: a specific, urgent problem — not a vague inconvenience — since audience and channel decisions downstream only make sense once the pain is concrete.
2. Segment beyond SMB/mid-market/enterprise: layer in growth potential (a fast-growing account deserves different treatment than a flat one), business model (B2B vs. platform vs. marketplace need fundamentally different motions), workload/traffic (a small team running huge infrastructure load belongs in "enterprise" regardless of headcount), and promote signal (visibility, strategic importance) — these are the axes that actually predict revenue, not org-chart tidiness.
3. Decide the PLG-vs-sales question deliberately and early: PLG is usually right at the beginning unless closing 8-figure deals from day one, but the most common mistake is waiting too long to add a sales motion — building a predictable outbound engine takes real time, and waiting until PLG growth slows creates a gap with no bridge.
4. Design the buying journey as a product experience end-to-end, from first awareness to a five-year retained customer — replace a boring discovery call with something that gives the prospect real value even before they buy (a collaborative architecture whiteboard, a peer benchmark), since technical differentiation narrows fast and the experience of being sold to becomes the actual differentiator.
5. Treat pricing as a product decision made during discovery, not an afterthought bolted on at launch: align price with where customers derive value and where the business incurs cost, and revisit pricing deliberately whenever product capabilities change materially rather than letting it drift stale.
6. Consider a GTM Engineer function for repetitive, legible GTM work (inbound qualification, research, follow-up, lost-deal analysis) — shadow top performers, codify their workflow, and build agents that increase leverage while keeping a human in the loop on anything sent externally.
7. Choose channels per the field guide's ranked strengths and traps: outbound (fast feedback, but a lead list can run dry), paid (most predictable at scale, but needs real targeting skill), PLG (low friction, but hard without a genuinely self-serve product), SEO (compounding but too slow for early validation), and sequence the founder's early tactics (friends, targeted strangers, press) before any of these longer engines are running.
8. Build the launch measurement plan around the value equation for anything priced at a premium: quantify the value delivered in dollar terms with the customer's own champion, and price at roughly a third of that value so the champion can justify the purchase internally.
9. Run the GTM organization to the "10 engineers, 10 minutes" litmus test — anyone leading launch GTM should have deep enough product knowledge that an engineer wouldn't immediately peg them as non-technical; the strongest GTM orgs feed customer signal back into product as fast as any other function.

## Inputs
- The specific, concrete pain the launch is meant to address (not a feature list)
- Candidate segments scored on growth potential, business model, and workload/traffic — not just size
- A current PLG-vs-sales-motion status, and how close the team is to a growth plateau that would make delaying sales risky
- Pricing hypotheses tied to quantified customer value, ready to validate with a real champion

## Output format
A stated pain and PACE-sequenced audience/channel plan; a segmentation table using the richer axes (size, growth potential, business model, workload/traffic, promote signal); an explicit PLG-vs-sales timing decision with the rationale for when a sales motion gets added; a mapped buying-journey experience (not just a funnel) with at least one deliberately designed high-value touchpoint; and a value-equation-based pricing hypothesis ready for validation with a real customer champion.

## Example
A B2B analytics tool launching a new tier segments not by company size but by data volume and growth trajectory — a small but fast-scaling startup with heavy usage gets enterprise-tier GTM treatment despite a 12-person headcount, since workload and growth potential predict revenue better than size alone. The team decides explicitly to layer in a light sales-assist motion now, before PLG growth plateaus, rather than waiting for a slowdown to force the decision reactively. The first sales touchpoint is a collaborative data-architecture walkthrough rather than a standard demo, giving the prospect something valuable regardless of outcome.

## Common pitfalls
- Segmenting only by company size, missing growth potential, workload, and promote signal — the axes that actually predict which accounts deserve premium GTM treatment.
- Waiting until PLG growth visibly slows before starting to build a sales motion, when building predictable outbound takes real time and the delay creates an unbridged growth gap.
- Treating the buying journey as a funnel to optimize conversion at each stage, instead of a designed experience — the highest-leverage differentiator once technical parity narrows is how the buying process itself feels.
