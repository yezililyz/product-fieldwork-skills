# Acquisition Channels

Grounded in "Acquisition Channel Strategy — The Distribution Engine Guide" (18 guests from Lenny's Podcast/Newsletter, including Brian Balfour, Elena Verna, Jason Cohen) and "Clay Mastery Playbook" for personalized-outbound execution.

## When to use
Use this when a team is spreading thin across four or five acquisition channels at once with none of them clearly working, or a founder is asking "which channel should we bet on" with no structured way to answer it. It's also the right tool when growth has stalled and the instinct is to ship another product tweak rather than recognize that the channel itself has saturated — Jason Cohen's "elephant curve," where channels rise, plateau, then sag as audiences saturate or the channel degrades. The signal to use it now: leadership is debating channels by opinion ("we should really be on TikTok") instead of by the Platform Trifecta test of customer need × company goals × channel strength.

## What it does
This skill runs a structured evaluation of which acquisition channel fits the business right now, using Brian Balfour's Growth Channel Dominance Matrix — the finding that each market tends to produce one winning channel, so the goal is becoming world-class at one rather than average at several — plus the seven structural distribution advantages a startup might already hold. It separates the tactics for a startup's first 1,000 users from the tactics for a scaled engine, and builds in the measurement discipline (MTA/MMM/CLS triangulation, payback period) to know honestly whether a channel is working.

## Method
1. Inventory which of the seven distribution advantages the company actually has: pre-existing audience, a unique viral loop, first-mover position on an emerging platform, a remarkable story, pre-existing strategic relationships, an early strategic partnership, or extraordinary hustle. Having two or three changes which channel is realistically viable.
2. Separate the "first 1,000 users" problem from the "scale engine" problem — different tactics apply. For the first 1,000, pick from the seven proven strategies (friends/colleagues, targeted strangers, going where users already hang out, FOMO/waitlists, niche influencers, press, or pre-launch viral content/community), starting from the concentric-circles-of-difficulty model: founder → friends → targeted strangers → audience hangouts → influencers → press → viral content → physical placement.
3. For the scale engine, apply the Platform Trifecta test to each candidate: does it match customer need, company goals, and the channel's own strength, DNA, and growth-curve stage? Only channels passing all three are viable long-term bets.
4. Commit to becoming world-class at ONE channel before layering a second, per the Growth Channel Dominance Matrix — splitting effort across several channels early usually means winning none of them.
5. If the business is B2B, sequence growth hiring by revenue motion: self-serve/PLG-first businesses hire growth before sales; sales-led businesses hire sales leadership first and add growth once product-led components already exist.
6. Layer paid acquisition only once it can serve one of its two legitimate jobs — profitable acquisition (gated behind a 5%+ free-to-paid conversion signal) or a fast messaging-learning engine — never as a patch for unproven product-market fit.
7. Build the measurement stack before scaling spend: triangulate MTA (daily, directional), MMM (2–4×/month, cross-channel budget calls), and CLS (quarterly, ground-truth calibration), and track payback period (CAC ÷ monthly contribution margin) as the north-star efficiency metric rather than CAC or LTV:CAC alone.
8. Watch for the channel's own lifecycle: expect the elephant-curve sag as a channel saturates, and have the next engine already identified before the current one plateaus, instead of reacting to the stall with incremental product fixes.

## Inputs
- The company's current growth stage (pre-1,000 users vs. scaling an existing base)
- Any of the seven distribution advantages the founder or team already holds
- Current channel mix and spend, if any, and whether the business is B2B or B2C
- Free-to-paid conversion rate and contribution margin, if paid acquisition is in scope

## Output format
A distribution-advantage inventory (which of the seven the company has); a first-1,000-users tactic recommendation, kept distinct from the scale-engine choice; a single recommended primary channel with the Platform Trifecta reasoning; a payback-period-based measurement plan; and an explicit note on when this channel is likely to saturate and what the second engine should be.

## Example
A B2B SaaS startup with no pre-existing audience but strong founder relationships in its industry (one of the seven distribution advantages) is scaling past its first 1,000 self-serve signups. The Trifecta test rules out paid acquisition — contribution margin is too thin at the current price point — and points to content/SEO as the primary engine, given the founder's domain credibility and an under-saturated search topic. Outbound sales, run through Clay for personalized enrichment, becomes the secondary channel for enterprise deals — sequenced only after the core content engine is running. Payback period, not CAC alone, is the tracked efficiency metric.

## Common pitfalls
- Running four channels at 25% effort each instead of committing to become world-class at one, which usually means losing all four to a competitor who picked one and went deep.
- Scaling paid spend to compensate for unproven product-market fit, creating a leaky bucket that looks like a channel problem but is actually a retention problem.
- Reacting to a stalled channel with incremental product fixes instead of recognizing channel saturation (the elephant-curve sag) and building the next distribution engine before the current one fully plateaus.
