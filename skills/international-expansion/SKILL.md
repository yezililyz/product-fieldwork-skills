# International Expansion

Grounded in "Going Global — The International Expansion Field Guide" and "Business Strategy — Creating Competitive Advantage" (Market Entry, Sizing & Sourcing).

## When to use
Use this when a company is planning to "launch in the UK" or "expand into Europe" as a single broad move, rather than sequencing city by city — launching a whole country dilutes supply and demand density into noise that never reaches liquidity anywhere. It's also the right tool when the readiness question ("should we expand yet") is being answered by ambition rather than by the three concrete signals that actually predict success: a stable flywheel, a defined PMF milestone, and real spare bandwidth. The signal to use it now: expansion is being planned as a single big-bang launch instead of a repeatable, city-by-city cycle.

## What it does
This skill sequences expansion through the Divide and Conquer Liquidity Cycle — constrain to one narrow geography, hit an explicit liquidity bar, then split or move to the next — after confirming the three readiness signals and picking target markets through a weighted, evidence-based scoring model rather than population alone. It also applies the "standardize the machine, localize the soul" principle: automate what can be automated (translation, ad copy) while insisting on real local empathy (trust norms, payment methods, boots on the ground) for what automation can't see. Once a market is chosen, it layers in the entry-mode spectrum (exporting, licensing/franchising, joint venture, wholly-owned subsidiary) as a separate, deliberate decision about how much commitment and control to take on — not just where and when to expand.

## Method
1. Confirm the three readiness signals before committing to any expansion: a stable flywheel (customers and supply function without constant manual hand-holding), a concrete milestone confirming PMF (an objective bar like 100 average daily orders — not vibes), and real spare bandwidth on the core team so expansion doesn't starve the original market.
2. Recognize the one exception to "wait for readiness": in a winner-take-all category under direct, fast-moving competitive attack (a well-funded copycat scaling in your target market), speed beats sequencing — move fast and accept imperfection, the way Airbnb did against a European copycat.
3. Pick markets with a weighted scoring model, not population alone: 3-5 attributes specific to the niche (search volume, existing supply density, regulatory friction), plus organic signals (waitlist signups, inbound traffic already arriving from that country) and ease of localization (language, payment methods).
4. Never launch a whole country — constrain to one city first (London, not the UK), since a country-wide launch dilutes both supply and demand density below the level needed to reach liquidity anywhere.
5. Define an explicit liquidity bar for that single city before anything else expands (e.g. a rolling 30-day GMV threshold, calibrated to the business) — nothing else proceeds until it's hit.
6. Once liquid, choose the next move deliberately: split the city into subregions and repeat the cycle, move to the next major city in the same country, or jump to a new country's primary city — then run the same constrain → achieve → review loop again, with each successive market expected to reach its milestone faster than the last.
7. Separate what to automate from what needs local empathy: automated translation and localization at scale (letting A/B-tested machine-translated copy win over "better" manual copy from local speakers, per Booking.com's data) works for language mechanics; local trust norms, payment method preferences, and cultural nuance need boots on the ground and local hires who can tune the product, not just translate it.
8. Pick the entry mode deliberately from the low-to-high commitment spectrum rather than defaulting to full ownership: exporting or a light remote presence (lowest risk, limited control) for an unproven market; licensing or a joint venture with a local partner once there's real signal but still-unknown local dynamics; a wholly-owned subsidiary only once accumulated evidence justifies the highest commitment and cost. Treat entry mode as a graduating decision, not a one-time choice — Starbucks entered China through licensees and joint ventures for six years, moved to progressive buyouts once viability was proven, and only took full ownership in 2017 once it needed complete control to protect its premium positioning at scale.
9. Audit the product for hardcoded domestic assumptions before entering a genuinely new market (currency formats, payment rails, regulatory fields) and build fraud/safety queues tuned to that market's specific abuse patterns before opening broad public access — international scaling introduces localized fraud patterns a domestic-only system was never built to catch.
10. Reject the "lite product" trap explicitly: international markets are not satisfied with a lower-quality version of the core product just because they're new — aim for quality parity, and find where local constraints actually create a genuine product advantage (unmappable markets, underserved payment needs) rather than an excuse to under-invest.

## Inputs
- Current flywheel health and whether a concrete PMF milestone has actually been hit in the home market
- Candidate target markets with real data on the 3-5 relevant niche attributes, not just population
- Any hardcoded domestic assumptions in the product (currency, payment, language) that need auditing before entry
- Local fraud/abuse pattern research specific to the target market, before opening broad access
- How much uncertainty still surrounds the target market, to place the entry-mode decision correctly on the exporting-to-wholly-owned commitment spectrum

## Output format
A readiness check against the three signals (or an explicit call that competitive urgency overrides them); a weighted market-scoring table ranking candidate markets on niche-specific attributes; a single-city launch plan with an explicit liquidity bar defined before launch; a split-or-move decision plan for after that bar is hit; an entry-mode recommendation (export/license/JV/wholly-owned) matched to current market uncertainty, with the evidence bar that would justify moving up the commitment ladder; and a named local-empathy investment (local hires, payment localization, fraud tuning) alongside whatever gets automated.

## Example
A marketplace considering "European expansion" instead picks its first target city (Berlin, not Germany) using a weighted score across existing organic traffic, supply density in food-delivery-adjacent categories, and payment-localization ease. The liquidity bar is set at €10K rolling 30-day GMV. Once hit, the team splits Berlin into two subregions rather than immediately jumping to Munich — the same cycle repeats, with the second subregion expected to reach its bar faster than the first did, before any decision is made about the next country.

## Common pitfalls
- Launching an entire country at once instead of a single constrained city, diluting supply and demand density below what's needed to reach liquidity anywhere and making it impossible to tell what's actually working.
- Selecting target markets by population size alone, missing the niche-specific attributes (existing supply density, regulatory friction, organic demand signal) that actually predict whether a market will convert.
- Treating international users as acceptable candidates for a stripped-down "lite" version of the product — the lite-product trap reliably backfires; local markets expect quality parity, not a downgrade.
- Defaulting to full ownership (or a joint venture) on day one regardless of how much is still unknown about the market, instead of treating entry mode as a graduating decision that starts low-commitment and earns higher control only as evidence accumulates.
