# Founder Sales

Grounded in "The Founder's Sales Playbook" (Og Mandino's scrolls + Rob Fitzpatrick's The Mom Test).

## When to use
Use this when a founder is running customer conversations that keep producing false positives — enthusiastic-sounding meetings, "I'd definitely buy that," compliments all around — and six months later there are still no paying customers. It's also the right tool when the actual blocker is showing up at all: dread before outreach, or a spiral of self-doubt after a run of "no"s. The signal to use it now: a founder describes a conversation as having "gone really well" but can't point to a single concrete commitment that came out of it.

## What it does
This skill applies the Mom Test's three rules for questions even a biased supporter can't lie about (talk about their life not your idea, ask about specifics in the past not opinions about the future, talk less and listen more), plus a strict five-phase conversation structure ending in a real ask for commitment — not a compliment. It pairs this with the daily-discipline half (Mandino's scrolls) for sustaining the volume of rejection-heavy outreach founder-led sales actually requires.

## Method
1. Never ask anyone whether your business is a good idea — not your mom, not friends, not early prospects. Ask about their life and their world instead; if you never mention your idea, they can't lie to flatter you about it.
2. Ask about specifics in the past, not hypotheticals about the future: "would you buy X" and "how much would you pay" are both bad questions that reliably produce optimistic, worthless answers — "tell me about the last time [relevant situation] happened" and "what have you tried already" are good questions that ground the conversation in real behavior.
3. Recognize and discard the three species of bad data in real time: compliments (worthless — "that meeting went great, everyone loves it" and six months later there are no customers), fluff (generic claims like "I usually..." or future-tense promises like "I would definitely buy that"), and unprompted feature requests (understand the motivation behind the request, don't just add it to a roadmap — people know their problems, not how to solve them).
4. Run the five-phase conversation structure: open by warming up on their world (2-3 min, not the problem yet), explore by digging into the problem space with past-tense questions, prioritize by asking what costs them the most (time, money, stress), advance only if far enough along (offer something concrete, ask for a real commitment, no hypotheticals), and always close with two questions — "who else should I talk to" and "is there anything else I should have asked."
5. Distinguish soft commitments (worthless: "that sounds interesting," "keep me in the loop") from real commitments (signal: a deposit, an intro scheduled today, a pilot with a named decision date, a signed letter of intent) — a great conversation that ends in vague enthusiasm is a failed conversation.
6. If far enough along, literally ask for money or a deposit — if you have it in hand, you know the interest was real; everything before that is a hypothesis, not validation.
7. Choose customers deliberately, not broadly: ask "who has this problem so severely they've already tried to solve it" — that segment is both your best early customer and your best teacher, worth more than a broad market that has the problem mildly.
8. Install the daily-discipline layer to sustain the volume this requires: schedule fixed conversation blocks and protect them like code sprints (not motivation-dependent), track "no"s as rungs climbed rather than evidence of failure, and run the rejection-reframe after each decline — what did I learn about their world, what assumption did this challenge, who should I talk to next.

## Inputs
- Access to real prospects for direct conversations, not just internal team or friendly advisors
- The specific problem hypothesis being tested, held loosely enough to abandon based on what's learned
- A tracking method for commitments (soft vs. real) across conversations, to avoid mistaking enthusiasm for validation
- Whatever psychological resistance is present (dread, rejection fatigue) alongside the tactical gap

## Output format
A conversation debrief per call: facts heard (not opinions), emotions flagged (strong reactions worth digging into), open questions still unanswered, and the specific next step committed to (an intro, a data-share, a scheduled pilot); a running commitment ladder (soft vs. real) across all conversations to date; and, where relevant, a persistence log tracking attempts before a breakthrough.

## Example
A founder's first five customer conversations all "went great" — enthusiastic nodding, "we'd definitely use something like this." Applying the bad-data filter reveals these are all compliments and fluff: no one has actually described a specific past instance of the problem, and no concrete ask was ever made. The next round of calls opens instead with "tell me about the last time this actually happened to you," ending each with a direct ask (a 30-day pilot with a named evaluation date). Three of five decline the ask outright — a worse-feeling but far more honest signal than the original five polite non-commitments.

## Common pitfalls
- Treating enthusiastic, compliment-heavy conversations as validation, when the Mom Test's core insight is that compliments are the most common and most worthless form of bad data.
- Asking hypothetical or future-tense questions ("would you buy this," "how much would you pay") and trusting the optimistic answers they reliably produce, instead of anchoring every question in specific past behavior.
- Letting a call end on vague enthusiasm without asking for any real commitment — a "great conversation" with no deposit, intro, or scheduled next step is, by the Mom Test's own definition, a failed one.
