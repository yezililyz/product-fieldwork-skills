# Founder Psychology

Grounded in "Effectuation — Book summary" (Saras Sarasvathy) and "The Founder's Sales Playbook" (Og Mandino's scrolls half).

## When to use
Use this when a founder is stuck trying to predict an unpredictable future — running market research for a category with no data, or waiting for a perfect plan before acting — when expert entrepreneurs facing the same uncertainty reason in the opposite direction: from means already at hand, not from a forecasted goal. It's also the right tool when the psychological blocker isn't strategic at all but simply showing up: dreading outreach, spiraling after a string of rejections, or losing discipline over a long, ambiguous build. The signal to use it now: a founder asks "what should I do to reach this goal?" when the honest first question is "what can I do with what I already have?"

## What it does
This skill applies Sarasvathy's empirical finding that 89% of expert entrepreneurs reason effectually (from means to emergent goals) rather than causally (from a fixed goal backward to means) — and gives the five effectuation principles as a practical decision logic for genuine uncertainty. It pairs this with Mandino's habit-installation method for the psychological discipline — showing up daily, treating rejection as neutral data, and persisting — that effectuation's iterative, commitment-driven process actually requires to sustain.

## Method
1. Diagnose which kind of uncertainty is actually in play: Knightian uncertainty (outcomes and probabilities are genuinely unknowable), goal ambiguity (you don't yet know your own goals beyond "succeed"), or isotropy (unclear which environmental factors even matter) — market research and causal planning don't work under any of these three, which is most early-stage founder reality.
2. Start from means, not goals: inventory identity (who you are — traits, tastes, abilities), knowledge (what you know — expertise, experience, the corridors only you've walked), and networks (whom you know) — then ask "what can I do with these?" and push further with "what else can I do?" before reaching for any predetermined goal.
3. Apply the affordable-loss principle to any risk decision: decide in advance the most you're willing to lose (time, money, reputation) and commit no more than that — rather than trying to compute an expected-return forecast for a genuinely unpredictable outcome. This is what licenses the plunge without needing to bet everything.
4. Apply the crazy-quilt principle to partnerships: negotiate with anyone willing to make a real commitment, without fixating on opportunity-cost analysis or competitive research — let whoever commits help set the goals of the venture, rather than recruiting people to a fixed plan.
5. Apply the lemonade principle to surprises: when something unexpected happens, don't defend the original plan against it — ask what new opportunity the surprise handed you, and stitch it into the venture. Surprises can be positive; effectuation treats contingency as raw material, not a threat.
6. Apply the pilot-in-the-plane principle: treat human action — your own steering and adjusting — as the prime driver of opportunity, rather than waiting on external trend forecasts or technology trajectories to reveal the "right" move.
7. Keep bets small and kill failures young, building a temporal portfolio of ventures rather than betting everything on one forecast — the fate of the founder isn't the fate of any single attempt.
8. Install the daily-discipline layer underneath all of the above: treat rejection as neutral data, not a verdict on your worth ("failure is alien to my life... unless I accept it"); build a fixed daily/weekly ritual for the hardest parts (outreach, hard conversations) so the work depends on habit, not motivation; and reframe each "no" with a fixed set of questions — what did I learn about their world, what assumption did this challenge, who should I talk to next.

## Inputs
- The founder's actual current means: identity, knowledge, and network — stated concretely, not aspirationally
- The specific decision or ambiguity causing the stuck feeling (a go/no-go call, a partnership, a pivot after a surprise)
- An honest affordable-loss number for the decision at hand (time, money, or reputation willing to risk)
- Whatever psychological blocker is present alongside the strategic one (dread, rejection spiral, loss of discipline)

## Output format
A means inventory (identity / knowledge / network) framed as "what can I do with these"; an explicit affordable-loss cap for the decision in front of the founder; a stated response to any recent surprise (defended against, or leveraged as the lemonade principle suggests); and, where a psychological blocker is present, a concrete daily-ritual commitment plus a rejection-reframe applied to the most recent "no."

## Example
A founder is stalled trying to "validate the market size" for a category with no comparable data — a causal question with no honest answer. Reframed effectually: their actual means are a decade of domain expertise (identity/knowledge) and three warm relationships with potential early customers (network). The affordable-loss cap is set at three months of part-time effort before any full commitment. Two of the three contacts decline; rather than treating this as a verdict, the founder runs the rejection-reframe (what did I learn, who should I talk to next) and asks each decliner for one referral — turning the "failure" into an expanding network rather than a stopping point.

## Common pitfalls
- Trying to forecast or market-research a genuinely novel, high-uncertainty opportunity instead of starting from means already at hand — the Curry-in-a-Hurry lesson: causal segmentation-targeting-positioning doesn't work when the market itself doesn't exist yet to be researched.
- Treating a string of rejections as evidence about personal worth or idea quality instead of neutral data to be mined ("what did this challenge, who should I talk to next") — this is precisely the trap the daily-discipline half of this skill exists to prevent.
- Defending the original plan against a surprise instead of asking what opportunity the surprise handed you — the lemonade principle is explicitly about treating positive contingency as fuel, not a deviation to correct back to plan.
