# Stakeholder Alignment

Grounded in "Anatomy of a Great PRD — Linear's Priority Micro-Adjust, Torn Down" and "Planning Cadence."

## When to use
Use this when a plan or spec is being shared without any explicit statement of who has a say, who actually decides, and who's just being informed — most bad alignment traces back to nobody having answered these questions before the discussion started, per the W Framework's core insight. It's also the right tool when a spec jumps straight to a proposed solution instead of first establishing the problem so thoroughly that stakeholders arrive at the same conclusion themselves — objections aren't pre-empted by better persuasion, they're pre-empted by a problem statement so concrete that the eventual solution feels inevitable.

## What it does
This skill applies the W Framework's explicit role split (Context from leadership → Plans from teams → Integration and prioritization by leadership → Buy-in before going wide) to any alignment effort, and borrows the real PRD teardown's craft moves — leading with the problem, decoding what stakeholders are actually asking for versus what they say, and showing rejected alternatives with real reasons — to write a spec that gets ahead of objections instead of triggering them.

## Method
1. Name explicit roles before circulating anything: who owns Context (the vision/goal/constraints), who's submitting Plans (the proposal), who does Integration (the actual prioritization and final call), and who just needs Buy-in visibility — skipping this is the single most common cause of alignment meetings that go in circles.
2. Lead any spec or proposal with the problem, not the solution — spend real weight naming the concrete failure modes of the current state before proposing anything, so the eventual fix feels like the obvious resolution of a tension you've already built, not an invented pitch.
3. Decode what stakeholders are literally asking for into what they actually need — when multiple stakeholders' requests cluster around a theme, treat the requests as symptoms and name the underlying disease, rather than building exactly what was asked for.
4. Show rejected alternatives explicitly, with reasons tied to principle or strategic fit, not just effort — a stakeholder who sees their suggested alternative addressed and rejected with real reasoning is far more likely to buy into the chosen path than one who never sees it discussed at all.
5. Ground every claim about stakeholder need in real evidence — an actual message thread, a direct quote, a concrete example — rather than a paraphrased summary; observation converts opinion into something stakeholders can't easily argue with.
6. Before the wider integration/decision step, run a genuine buy-in pass with the people most affected: share the near-final plan, explain the trade-offs and the reasoning behind rejected alternatives, and expect (and budget time for) real pushback — skipping this step to save time reliably costs far more goodwill later than the time it would have taken.
7. Concentrate the actual decision-making conversation on real disagreement, not on re-litigating what everyone already agrees on — most alignment friction lives in a small number of genuine open questions, not the 80% of a plan nobody actually contests.
8. Close any alignment discussion with an explicit statement of what was decided and by whom — an alignment conversation that ends without a named decision-maker's call recorded will quietly get re-opened by whoever wasn't fully satisfied.

## Inputs
- The specific plan or spec needing stakeholder alignment, and the current (implicit or explicit) understanding of who decides
- Any indirect or literal stakeholder requests that need decoding into their underlying need
- Real evidence (messages, quotes, concrete examples) backing the stated problem, rather than a paraphrased summary
- The specific alternative(s) stakeholders might expect and want addressed, even if ultimately rejected

## Output format
An explicit role map (who owns Context, who submits Plans, who does Integration, who just needs Buy-in) for this specific alignment effort; a problem-first framing (concrete failure modes before any proposed fix); a decoded underlying need where stakeholder requests cluster around a theme; a list of considered-and-rejected alternatives with principle-based reasons; and a recorded final decision with the specific decision-maker named.

## Example
Three stakeholders each request a different reporting feature. Instead of picking one request to build, the underlying need across all three is decoded as "give account owners visibility into which team members haven't completed onboarding" — a single feature serving all three literal requests. The resulting spec explicitly shows the two more elaborate alternatives that were considered and rejected (a full analytics dashboard, a per-user audit log), each with a stated reason tied to scope and product identity, before presenting the chosen lightweight solution — giving stakeholders confidence their request was heard even where the literal ask wasn't built.

## Common pitfalls
- Circulating a plan with no explicit statement of who actually decides, producing an alignment "discussion" that never converges because nobody agreed in advance who has the final call.
- Jumping straight to a proposed solution without first establishing the problem concretely enough that stakeholders would reach the same conclusion — inviting objections that better problem-framing would have pre-empted.
- Skipping the buy-in step to save time, sharing a finished plan only after it's locked — this reliably costs more trust and rework than the time saved by skipping it.
