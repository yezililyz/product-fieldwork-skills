# Stakeholder Alignment

Grounded in "Anatomy of a Great PRD — Linear's Priority Micro-Adjust, Torn Down" and "Planning Cadence," plus a standalone stakeholder-mapping and pre-wiring framework (not sourced from a specific yezi-hub guide) for politically sensitive decisions.

## When to use
Use this when a plan or spec is being shared without any explicit statement of who has a say, who actually decides, and who's just being informed — most bad alignment traces back to nobody having answered these questions before the discussion started, per the W Framework's core insight. It's also the right tool when a spec jumps straight to a proposed solution instead of first establishing the problem so thoroughly that stakeholders arrive at the same conclusion themselves. Reach for the stakeholder-mapping half specifically before any recommendation goes into a formal decision meeting where multiple stakeholders with different interests, incentives, and levels of influence will weigh in — especially when the decision is politically sensitive (a reorg, a resource reallocation, a change with visible winners and losers) and the natural instinct is to walk into the room and let the analysis speak for itself. The signal to use it now: someone says "the logic is solid, we should be fine" about a decision that visibly threatens at least one powerful stakeholder's territory, or past proposals of a similar kind have died in exactly that kind of meeting despite strong analytical merit.

## What it does
This skill applies the W Framework's explicit role split (Context from leadership → Plans from teams → Integration and prioritization by leadership → Buy-in before going wide) to any alignment effort, and borrows the real PRD teardown's craft moves — leading with the problem, decoding what stakeholders are actually asking for versus what they say, and showing rejected alternatives with real reasons — to write a spec that gets ahead of objections instead of triggering them. For higher-stakes or politically sensitive decisions, it goes further: identifying every stakeholder whose explicit or tacit support the decision requires, mapping them by influence and interest to prioritize where effort should go, and building a specific, sequenced engagement plan to move swing votes into support before the decision is ever formally put to the room.

## Method
1. Name explicit roles before circulating anything: who owns Context (the vision/goal/constraints), who's submitting Plans (the proposal), who does Integration (the actual prioritization and final call), and who just needs Buy-in visibility — skipping this is the single most common cause of alignment meetings that go in circles.
2. Lead any spec or proposal with the problem, not the solution — spend real weight naming the concrete failure modes of the current state before proposing anything, so the eventual fix feels like the obvious resolution of a tension you've already built, not an invented pitch. Decode what stakeholders are literally asking for into what they actually need — when multiple stakeholders' requests cluster around a theme, treat the requests as symptoms and name the underlying disease, rather than building exactly what was asked for.
3. Show rejected alternatives explicitly, with reasons tied to principle or strategic fit, not just effort, and ground every claim about stakeholder need in real evidence — an actual message thread, a direct quote, a concrete example — rather than a paraphrased summary.
4. For a politically sensitive or high-stakes decision, widen the lens: list every stakeholder who has either formal decision authority or informal influence over those who do — don't limit the list to the org chart's obvious approvers. Map each on a 2x2 of influence versus interest to decide where engagement effort concentrates: high-influence, high-interest stakeholders need direct, early engagement; high-influence, low-interest stakeholders need brief, well-timed awareness rather than a full pitch.
5. Classify each key stakeholder's current position as a firm supporter, a firm opponent, or a genuine swing vote, and concentrate the engagement plan on swing votes — firm supporters need reinforcement, not persuasion, and firm opponents are rarely converted by more of the same argument that already failed to move them. Write a short motivation analysis for each: what outcome are they actually trying to protect or achieve, and what specifically do they fear this decision will cost them — objections voiced in a meeting are often a proxy for this underlying motivation, not the real issue.
6. Design a specific engagement card for each swing-vote stakeholder — the tailored message that speaks to their actual motivation, the right channel (one-on-one versus group briefing), and the right timing relative to the formal decision — and sequence the plan deliberately: engage stakeholders whose support will make it easier to bring others along first, saving anyone likely to be reassured by seeing others already on board for later in the sequence.
7. Run a pre-mortem on the formal decision meeting itself: imagine it has just gone badly, work backward to identify which stakeholder raised the objection that derailed it and why it wasn't defused beforehand, then close the specific gap the pre-mortem revealed before the room convenes.
8. Before the wider integration/decision step, run a genuine buy-in pass with the people most affected: share the near-final plan, explain the trade-offs and the reasoning behind rejected alternatives, and expect (and budget time for) real pushback — skipping this step to save time reliably costs far more goodwill later. Concentrate the actual decision-making conversation on real disagreement, not on re-litigating what everyone already agrees on, and close with an explicit statement of what was decided and by whom — an alignment conversation that ends without a named decision-maker's call recorded will quietly get re-opened by whoever wasn't fully satisfied.

## Inputs
- The specific plan or spec needing stakeholder alignment, and the current (implicit or explicit) understanding of who decides
- Any indirect or literal stakeholder requests that need decoding into their underlying need, and real evidence backing the stated problem
- For politically sensitive decisions: a list of stakeholders with formal or informal influence, and any known history of this group's past reactions to similar decisions
- The date and format of the formal decision meeting

## Output format
An explicit role map (Context / Plans / Integration / Buy-in) for this specific alignment effort; a problem-first framing with a decoded underlying need where requests cluster around a theme; a list of considered-and-rejected alternatives with principle-based reasons. For higher-stakes decisions, add: an influence-vs-interest stakeholder map with each person's current position (support/oppose/swing) marked; a motivation analysis and engagement card per swing vote; a sequenced pre-wire plan; and a pre-mortem of the formal decision meeting identifying the most likely failure point and how it's being defused in advance. Close with a recorded final decision naming the specific decision-maker.

## Example
Three stakeholders each request a different reporting feature; instead of picking one to build, the underlying need is decoded as "give account owners visibility into which team members haven't completed onboarding," and the spec shows two more elaborate alternatives that were considered and rejected, each with a stated reason, before presenting the chosen lightweight solution. On a more contested reorganization proposal, the stakeholder map identifies two swing votes — a regional VP who fears losing direct reports, and a finance lead whose real, previously unstated concern is complicated cost-allocation reporting — and sequences a one-on-one with the finance lead first, addressing the reporting concern directly, before a one-on-one with the regional VP once the finance lead is on board; the pre-mortem flags that without this sequence, the VP would likely raise the objection publicly and pull other undecided attendees along, exactly what the pre-wire is designed to prevent.

## Common pitfalls
- Circulating a plan with no explicit statement of who actually decides, producing an alignment "discussion" that never converges, or jumping straight to a proposed solution without first establishing the problem concretely enough that stakeholders would reach the same conclusion.
- Skipping the buy-in step to save time, sharing a finished plan only after it's locked — this reliably costs more trust and rework than the time saved.
- Treating a stated objection in the meeting as the real issue rather than digging into the underlying motivation beforehand, or spending equal engagement effort across all stakeholders instead of concentrating on genuine swing votes.
- Walking into a politically sensitive decision meeting without having run a pre-mortem, so the first time an objection surfaces is live, in front of the full group, with no prepared response.
