# War Gaming

A standalone strategy framework — not sourced from a specific yezi-hub guide. Unlike the other skills in this library, this one isn't grounded in a real Product Fieldwork guide; it's included as a general-purpose consulting methodology worth having on hand.

## When to use
Use this before committing to a strategy move that will draw a visible, consequential reaction from competitors, customers, or regulators — a price change, a major launch, an aggressive market entry, an acquisition — and especially when the current plan has been built and reviewed only from the company's own perspective, with competitor reaction treated as a footnote rather than a modeled scenario. It's also the right tool when a past launch was blindsided by a competitor response that, in hindsight, was fairly predictable. The signal to use it now: the strategy document has a one-line "competitors may respond" risk entry with no actual analysis of how, and no pre-planned counter-response. Without structured simulation, organizations plan as if the world holds still after their move, and are then surprised — expensively and publicly — when it doesn't.

## What it does
This skill runs a structured simulation in which separate roles argue and act from the perspective of your organization, your competitors, and other affected external parties, playing out multiple rounds of move and countermove to surface second- and third-order effects a single-perspective plan would never generate. It closes by registering the specific failure modes the simulation exposed and pre-committing the actual response your organization will take if each one starts to materialize in reality.

## Method
1. Design the scenario precisely: state the specific strategic move under test and the exact question the game needs to answer (for example, "does our launch pricing survive a competitor price response," not a vague "stress-test the strategy").
2. Assemble a structured, multi-role team: a team representing your own organization's actual plan and decision logic, one or more teams representing key rival competitors (playing to that rival's real incentives and historical behavior, not a caricature of them), a team representing other external actors whose reaction matters (customers, regulators, suppliers, partners), and a neutral control function that referees the round, enforces realistic constraints, and adjudicates ambiguous outcomes.
3. Brief the rival-playing team specifically on that competitor's actual cost structure, capital position, and historical pattern of behavior — a rival team that isn't grounded in real competitive intelligence will produce unrealistic, either too-passive or too-aggressive, moves that don't actually stress-test the plan.
4. Play the game in discrete rounds (typically three), where each round consists of your team's move, the other roles' reactions, and the control function's ruling on the resulting new state of the market before the next round begins.
5. In each round, explicitly force second- and third-order effects rather than stopping at the first reaction — after a rival's price cut, force the round to also address how customers and your own sales team behave in response to that price cut, not just how you would counter-price.
6. Watch specifically for the round in which the original plan's core economic or strategic logic breaks — the point where an assumption in the business case (a volume estimate, a margin assumption, a customer retention assumption) no longer holds given the simulated competitive reaction. Name that breaking point explicitly; it is usually the single most valuable output of the exercise.
7. Register every failure mode surfaced during play as a discrete, named risk — not folded into a single vague "competitive risk" line — each with the specific trigger condition that would signal it's starting to happen in the real market.
8. For each registered failure mode, pre-commit a specific response and the threshold that triggers it, decided calmly now rather than improvised later under real competitive pressure; avoid the common failure of running an engaging simulation that surfaces sharp insights but produces no concrete change to the actual go-forward plan.

## Inputs
- The strategy or launch plan under test, including its core economic assumptions
- Competitive intelligence on the key rivals to be simulated, including cost structure and historical behavior patterns
- A picture of other relevant external actors (customers, regulators, key suppliers or partners) and their likely interests
- Participants able to credibly play each role, ideally including people who know the represented rival or actor well

## Output format
A stated scenario and the specific question the game answered; a round-by-round record of moves, reactions, and the resulting market state; an explicit statement of the round and mechanism at which the original plan's core assumption broke, if it did; a named list of failure modes with their trigger conditions; and a pre-committed response plan mapped to each trigger.

## Example
A company war-games an aggressive market-entry launch with an assumed 25% first-year share target. Playing the rival role grounded in the incumbent's actual cost structure, the simulation shows the incumbent has both the margin cushion and a public-market incentive to defend share aggressively, and the rival team responds to the launch with a matching price cut within the simulated first quarter. By round two, the control function rules that the original plan's margin assumption no longer holds under the incumbent's realistic response, breaking the business case as built. The pre-committed response registered: if the incumbent's price cut materializes within 90 days of actual launch (the trigger), the company will shift from a price-led entry to a bundled-value entry instead of matching price, a decision made calmly in the simulation rather than reactively in the market.

## Common pitfalls
- Playing the rival role as a passive or unrealistic actor instead of grounding it in that competitor's actual incentives and historical behavior, which produces a simulation too easy to "win."
- Stopping analysis at the first-round reaction instead of forcing second- and third-order effects, missing the compounding dynamics that actually break most plans.
- Running an insightful simulation but failing to convert its findings into pre-committed, trigger-based responses, so the same failure mode still catches the organization off guard when it happens for real.
