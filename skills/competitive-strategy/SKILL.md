# Competitive Strategy

Grounded in "Business Strategy — Creating Competitive Advantage."

## When to use
Use this when a competitive analysis is really just a features-comparison spreadsheet with no framework connecting the dots to an actual strategic recommendation. It's also the right tool when a proposed advantage sounds strong in a meeting but hasn't been checked against whether a competitor can simply copy it — the difference between something merely good and something strategically unique (VRIN-passing). The signal to use it now: a strategy deck lists "strengths" and "opportunities" as two disconnected bullet lists with no explicit pairing showing which strength captures which opportunity.

## What it does
This skill runs the frameworks sequentially rather than as four disconnected outputs stapled together: PESTEL scans the macro-environment, Five Forces explains why the specific industry is structurally profitable or not, VRIN tests whether a candidate resource or capability is actually defensible, and SWOT's SO/WO/ST/WT pairings convert the analysis into 2-3 prioritized actions. It also covers reading competitors' annual reports for the strategy hidden in the numbers, and the Ansoff/Blue Ocean tools for deciding how and where to grow next.

## Method
1. Define the strategic decision before scanning anything — market entry, defending an advantage, a growth bet — since an unfocused PESTEL/Five Forces scan produces a list nobody acts on.
2. Run PESTEL on the 2-3 factors per category (political, economic, social, technological, environmental, legal) that actually change the decision at hand — delete any row that wouldn't change what you'd decide.
3. Run Porter's Five Forces on the specific industry to explain the structural profitability question PESTEL can't answer: rivalry, new entrants, supplier power, buyer power, and substitutes.
4. Gather competitive intelligence ethically from public sources only (investor relations, news, job postings signaling capability investments, industry reports), and separate signal (patterns repeating across markets, backed by data over time) from noise (one-off events, media hype).
5. Test every candidate resource or capability against VRIN — Valuable, Rare, Inimitable, Non-substitutable. A resource passing all four is a core competency and a real defensible advantage; failing even one makes it merely useful, not strategically unique — and remember sustainability isn't automatic, since a rare capability today may not stay rare.
6. Build the SWOT grid from the internal analysis (S/W) and the PESTEL/Five Forces findings (O/T), then do the actual strategic work: pair every strength with an opportunity it could capture (SO), every weakness with the opportunity it blocks (WO), every strength with a threat it defends against (ST), and every weakness with its greatest exposure (WT) — then prioritize the 2-3 pairings that matter most.
7. Read competitors' annual reports for what the narrative doesn't say: a segment growing faster than the letter mentions, a declining segment given little explanation, or a capex jump right before an announcement — risk disclosures in particular reveal exactly where a competitor feels pressure.
8. Choose a growth path only after the above: Ansoff Matrix for product/market direction (penetration, development, diversification), portfolio tools (BCG/GE-McKinsey) for capital allocation discipline across bets, and Blue Ocean's four actions (eliminate/reduce/raise/create) when the goal is escaping head-to-head competition on the same value curve entirely rather than winning it.

## Inputs
- The specific strategic decision this analysis needs to inform (entry, defense, growth, exit)
- Public sources on named competitors (filings, news, hiring signals, industry reports)
- The company's own candidate resources/capabilities to test against VRIN
- Any existing SWOT or positioning work already done, to check for un-paired quadrants

## Output format
A stated strategic decision the analysis serves; a 2-3-row-per-category PESTEL and Five Forces summary scoped to that decision; a VRIN verdict (core competency / non-core) on each candidate resource; a SWOT grid with explicit SO/WO/ST/WT pairings, prioritized to the top 2-3; and, where relevant, a named growth-path recommendation (Ansoff quadrant or Blue Ocean four-actions grid).

## Example
A company defending its market position runs PESTEL and finds a technological factor has flipped from advantage to threat — the streaming infrastructure that once differentiated it is now table stakes industry-wide. Five Forces confirms rivalry and new-entrant threat have both intensified as a result. VRIN testing shows the original tech advantage no longer passes (no longer rare), but original content and subscriber behavioral data do pass all four criteria — the defensible moat has moved. The SWOT SO pairing recommends anchoring an ad-supported tier on the still-defensible originals engine, rather than trying to re-compete on infrastructure that's no longer a real advantage.

## Common pitfalls
- Running PESTEL as an unfocused checklist-filling exercise instead of starting from the specific decision it needs to inform, producing a long list nobody acts on.
- Calling something a competitive advantage because it's good, without testing it against VRIN — a resource that's merely valuable and rare but easily copied (not inimitable) isn't actually defensible.
- Stapling four frameworks' outputs together in parallel instead of running them sequentially, so each one's finding becomes the next framework's question — the sequence is where the real insight compounds.
