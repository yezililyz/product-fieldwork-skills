# Defining ICP

Grounded in "Go-to-Market, Pricing & Sales — The Field Guide."

## When to use
Use this when an ICP is defined by firmographics alone ("mid-market SaaS companies, 50-500 employees") with no test for whether that segment actually feels the pain the product solves, or whether they can afford to buy. It's also the right tool when a team is chasing an audience or a channel before nailing down the pain — the reliable way founders stay stuck under $1M ARR for years is starting anywhere in the pain-audience-channel-expansion sequence except pain. The signal to use it now: sales is closing deals but retention and expansion are weak, suggesting the ICP was defined by who could be reached, not by who actually needed the product urgently.

## What it does
This skill defines ICP as the second step of the PACE sequence (Pain → Audience → Channel → Expansion), applying a three-trait test to any candidate segment: do they feel the pain urgently, do they have the money, and do they face an expensive cost of inaction. It explicitly rejects starting from audience or channel, since the pain has to be established and specific before "who has this pain" becomes an answerable question.

## Method
1. Establish the pain first, before defining any audience trait — an urgent, painful, specific problem, not a vague inconvenience or a "nice to have." Starting audience-first ("let's target this niche") without a defined pain produces a segment definition with nothing to test it against.
2. Test each candidate segment against the three ICP traits: do they feel the pain (it's urgent and showing up now, not hypothetical), do they have the money (already paying meaningfully for adjacent software, not a segment that would need to find new budget), and do they face an expensive cost of inaction (the bigger the cost of not acting, the easier the sale).
3. Prioritize segments with a high cost of inaction specifically — this trait predicts fast sales cycles, high retention, active feedback, and organic referrals, since these buyers adopt because they must, not because they were persuaded.
4. Explicitly avoid the inverse: segments with low or no cost of inaction convert slowly if at all, and even when they do convert, retaining them burns disproportionate account-management effort just to keep them from churning.
5. Once the ICP is defined by pain-fit, layer in the segmentation axes that actually change how they buy: size alone is insufficient — growth potential, business model (B2B vs. B2C vs. platform), and workload/traffic often matter more than headcount (a small team running huge infrastructure load belongs in "enterprise" segmentation regardless of size).
6. Validate ICP fit continuously against actual conversion and retention data, not just initial firmographic assumptions — an ICP is a hypothesis until real customers in that segment prove out fast cycles and high retention.
7. Once ICP is validated, let it drive downstream pricing and channel decisions rather than defining those independently — a high-cost-of-inaction ICP can typically absorb premium, value-based pricing; a low-cost-of-inaction segment usually can't.

## Inputs
- The specific, named pain the product solves — stated concretely, not as a category ("saves time on X" is not a pain; "prevents Y specific costly failure" is)
- Candidate segments to test against the three ICP traits
- Any existing customer data showing which segments actually convert quickly and retain well
- Current spend/budget signals for candidate segments (are they already paying for adjacent tools at a comparable price point?)

## Output format
A stated, specific pain (not a category); a scored table of candidate segments against the three traits (feels the pain / has the money / expensive cost of inaction); a prioritized ICP recommendation with the strongest cost-of-inaction rationale; and a note on which segments to explicitly deprioritize despite surface-level fit (e.g. "matches firmographically but low cost of inaction").

## Example
A compliance-automation tool considers three candidate segments: fast-growing fintech startups, established mid-market banks, and small local credit unions. Fintech startups feel the pain acutely (regulatory deadlines are real and imminent) and have real budget, but a missed deadline's cost is often just a fine they can absorb — moderate cost of inaction. Mid-market banks face the same deadlines with materially higher fines and reputational cost — high cost of inaction — becoming the prioritized ICP, even though the fintech segment looked like an easier initial sales cycle on paper.

## Common pitfalls
- Defining ICP by firmographics (size, industry, region) without testing whether the segment actually feels the target pain urgently — a demographically clean segment with no real urgency converts slowly no matter how well-targeted the marketing is.
- Chasing the audience most reachable through an existing channel instead of the audience that actually has the pain — this is the "wrong end of PACE" mistake that keeps startups stuck for years.
- Prioritizing a segment with real pain and real budget but negligible cost of inaction — it looks viable on two of three traits, but weak cost of inaction predicts long sales cycles and expensive-to-retain customers.
