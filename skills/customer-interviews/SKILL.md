# Customer Interviews

Grounded in "Continuous Discovery Habits • Teresa Torres" and "Discovery in the Age of AI — Teresa Torres."

## When to use
Use this when interview notes are full of opinions and hypotheticals ("I'd probably use that," "I think I'd like it if...") but nothing about what people actually, concretely did — the tell that the interview technique itself is the problem, not the participants. It's also the right tool when a team is about to run a "would you use this?" prototype test, since honest yes-answers from real people still reliably fail to predict actual usage. The signal to use it now: an interview produces general impressions instead of one specific, narratable story.

## What it does
This skill applies story-based interviewing: instead of asking people to predict or generalize, it collects one reliable story of what they actually did recently, using temporal prompts to excavate the narrative rather than guesses that contaminate it with the interviewer's own assumptions. It also covers turning each interview into a one-page snapshot that feeds directly into a team's broader opportunity-tracking practice.

## Method
1. Skip the two weak tiers of interview question entirely: Tier 1 ("here's a prototype, would you use this?") and Tier 2 ("tell me about your experience with the product") both produce what people think or predict, not what they actually did.
2. Open with the Tier 3 question: "tell me about the last time you [solved the problem this product/feature addresses]" — a specific, recent, real story is the target, not a general impression.
3. Situate the person back in the actual moment before asking anything else: "so it was after dinner / at your desk — let's go back to that moment, walk me through it."
4. Excavate the story using only temporal prompts — "what did you do first," "what came next," "walk me through going from X to Y," "what device were you using at that point" — and never guess at what might have happened ("did something go wrong?" "did you check the recommendations?"). Guessing is the single most common interviewing mistake and it projects the interviewer's assumptions into the participant's story.
5. Deliberately break the normal 50/50 conversational norm: the participant should do most of the talking. If you're talking as much as they are, you're leading, not excavating.
6. Keep the interview free of solution talk entirely — no "would you use this," no like/dislike ratings, no product opinions. This is a listening session about a real past experience, not a validation session.
7. Capture the interview immediately afterward in a one-page snapshot: an experience map of the story's key moments (for pattern-spotting across interviews), every opportunity (unmet need, pain point, desire) heard, a handful of quick segmenting facts, one memorable quote, and any insights not yet actionable, parked rather than lost.
8. After 3-4 interviews on the same topic, look across snapshots for the story that recurs — the same moment, the same friction point — since a single interview is an anecdote, but a pattern across several is a real signal worth acting on.

## Inputs
- Access to real users or customers who've recently done the thing being studied
- A stated topic or outcome the interviews are meant to inform (not a solution to validate)
- A way to record or take detailed notes during the session, since the snapshot depends on capturing specifics
- Enough interviews (3-4 minimum) to look for cross-interview patterns rather than acting on one anecdote

## Output format
A completed one-page interview snapshot per session (experience map, opportunities list, quick facts, salient quote, parked insights); a note on which Tier-3 opening question was used; and, after 3-4 interviews, a short cross-interview pattern summary naming the story or friction point that recurred.

## Example
A team studying checkout abandonment opens with "tell me about the last time you almost bought something online but didn't finish." One participant's story, excavated with temporal prompts rather than guesses, reveals they abandoned specifically at the shipping-cost reveal step — a concrete moment, not a vague "I don't like surprises." The snapshot captures this as an opportunity under a "reviewing total cost" branch; after three more interviews recover the same specific moment, it becomes the team's next target opportunity instead of a guessed-at redesign of the whole checkout flow.

## Common pitfalls
- Asking a hypothetical or prediction question ("would you use this?") and treating an honest, positive answer as validation — it isn't; people are bad at predicting their own future behavior.
- Guessing at what happened in the story ("did X go wrong?") instead of using neutral temporal prompts, which contaminates the interview with the interviewer's own assumptions about the problem.
- Letting the interview drift into solution talk or opinions ("what would you want to see instead?") instead of staying strictly in the lane of a real past experience.
