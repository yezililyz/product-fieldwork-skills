# Goal Setting & OKRs

Grounded in "Planning Cadence."

## When to use
Use this when OKRs are set once a year in isolation from the planning process that's supposed to execute them — producing goals nobody references again until the next annual cycle. It's also the right tool when a team is unsure how much planning ceremony its size and stage actually warrants — the same rigid quarterly OKR process that serves a 500-person company can smother a 20-person team still searching for product-market fit. The signal to use it now: OKRs exist on a slide but nobody can say, in a given week, what they're doing to move toward one.

## What it does
This skill sets goals at the altitude appropriate to company stage (annual company priorities down to weekly momentum checks), using the W Framework's clear division of labor (leadership sets vision/goal/final prioritization; teams propose plans and flag risk) to avoid the ambiguity that wrecks most goal-setting. It calibrates goal rigidity to volatility — rolling, confidence-tiered roadmaps for steady-state planning, and thematic "seasons" replacing rigid OKRs entirely in genuinely volatile spaces.

## Method
1. Match planning altitude to company stage before setting any goal structure: pre-PMF teams under 20 people should run two-week sprints with no formal OKRs at all ("what are we doing today, this week?"); 100+ person teams with PMF plan in one-to-two-quarter horizons; 500+ person multi-product orgs run quarterly planning with bi-annual company OKRs.
2. Apply the 10% planning rule: spend no more than 10% of the execution period actually planning it (a one-week sprint gets half a day; a quarter gets about a week) — if planning is eating meaningfully more, the strategy underneath is missing, not the process.
3. Use the W Framework's role clarity to structure the back-and-forth: leadership owns an inspiring vision, a concrete goal for the period, and the final prioritization call; teams own the execution plans, surfacing gaps/risks, and committing to a goal at a given resource level — most bad planning traces back to nobody having answered who does what, when.
4. Draft the company strategy leadership shares first with five explicit ingredients: mission, vision, goal, a short strategy narrative, and 3-5 strategic pillars — explicitly marked as an invitation with "what we're sure of / what we assume / what we need help answering," not a mandate.
5. Concentrate resources on 3-5 bets maximum during the integration step, roughly 80% safe short-term and 20% risky long-term — cut good ideas that aren't perishable, strategic, or differentiating rather than spreading thin across everything.
6. Build in a real buy-in step before going wide: share the integrated plan with team leads first, explain the why and trade-offs, expect 5-10% of the plan to change from their pushback, then commit — skipping this step burns hundreds of hours of team goodwill later.
7. For anything beyond the current quarter, use confidence-tiered rolling roadmaps rather than one fixed annual plan: ~80% confidence/full detail for the current quarter (a real commitment), ~50% for next quarter (explicit permission to pivot), and themes only beyond that.
8. In genuinely volatile domains, replace rigid time-boxed OKRs with "seasons": define the season from the secular change driving it, set a North Star metric, keep OKRs loose and quarterly, execute in 4-6 week squad goals, and deliberately leave slack for the unplanned.
9. Bridge quarterly/annual goals to daily execution with a lightweight weekly ritual — a single recurring question ("what are we doing this week to get closer to our strategic goal?") posted async, which does more to keep OKRs alive than any dashboard review.

## Inputs
- Company stage/headcount, to select the right planning altitude and cadence
- The current draft company strategy (mission/vision/goal/pillars), if one exists, to check it against the five-ingredient template
- How volatile the operating environment currently is, to decide between rigid OKRs and a thematic "seasons" approach
- Whatever weekly ritual (or absence of one) currently bridges quarterly goals to daily work

## Output format
A stated planning altitude and cadence matched to company stage; a company strategy draft with the five ingredients (mission, vision, goal, strategy narrative, 3-5 pillars) marked explicit vs. assumed; a resourcing split showing the 3-5 concentrated bets (not a long list); a confidence-tiered roadmap view (current quarter high-confidence, next quarter directional, beyond that thematic); and a defined weekly ritual connecting the goal to that week's actual work.

## Example
A 40-person startup has been running rigid quarterly OKRs modeled on a much larger company's process, and planning now eats three weeks of every quarter with goals nobody references after week one. Matched against company stage, this is over-processed — the team drops to one-to-two-quarter planning horizons with a lighter W-Framework pass (leadership drafts a short strategy narrative, teams propose plans against it in one week), and reintroduces a simple Monday async check-in ("what are we doing this week toward the quarter's goal") as the actual behavior-driving mechanism, rather than relying on the quarterly OKR doc to do that job on its own.

## Common pitfalls
- Running the same rigid, high-ceremony OKR process regardless of company stage — a process built for 500 people can smother a 20-person team still searching for product-market fit, and the fix is calibrating cadence to stage, not abandoning goals altogether.
- Setting OKRs once a year with no weekly bridge connecting them to actual work, so the goals exist on a slide but never shape a single week's priorities.
- Planning every future quarter at the same level of detail as the current one — full-resolution planning for a quarter two cycles out wastes real effort on speculation that will change anyway; confidence should decrease explicitly the further out the horizon.
