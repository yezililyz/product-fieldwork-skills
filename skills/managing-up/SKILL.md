# Managing Up

Grounded in "The PM Operating Guide" and "Courage as a Service — Vanessa Lee, Shopify."

## When to use
Use this when an update to leadership reads as a flat list of activity with no traceable link back to strategy — the most common documentation mistake is writing Vision, Roadmap, and PRD as independent deliverables instead of a hierarchy where each answers a different question and descends from the one above. It's also the right tool when a PM has real evidence that a leadership-endorsed direction is wrong, but hesitates to say so — Vanessa Lee's framing of courage ("taking action in the presence of fear") is specifically about the moment of saying "we worked on this for three months, but I don't think it's right," not about seizing authority. The signal to use it now: a stakeholder update leaves leadership unable to trace a specific ask back to the strategic pillar it serves.

## What it does
This skill structures any update or document as a decision hierarchy (why → what → how → when), so a roadmap or PRD traces cleanly back to the strategy that justifies it, and applies a reusable eight-question thinking checklist before writing anything. For the harder case — pushing back on leadership itself — it applies the "unfinished-artifact" tactic (an unshakeable, evidence-backed claim paired with a deliberately unpolished proposed fix that invites collaboration rather than defensiveness) and the "courage, not authority" framing that makes pushback land as partnership rather than a power move.

## Method
1. Answer the eight-question thinking checklist in order before writing any update: why (what problem), for whom, what outcomes matter, what principles must always hold, which capabilities are required, what's the build sequence, what exactly ships next, and how will success be measured.
2. Structure every document as a hierarchy, not a flat list: Vision (3-5 year destination, rarely mentions features) → Strategy (pillars, how the vision gets achieved) → Roadmap (which capabilities ship in what order) → PRD (one coherent capability) → Epics/Stories. If a PRD or roadmap item can't be traced back up to a strategic pillar, that's the signal the hierarchy was skipped.
3. Match update cadence to each document's actual time horizon: vision rarely changes (years), strategy revisits every 6-12 months, roadmap refreshes quarterly, PRDs per initiative — don't rewrite the vision every quarter just because the roadmap did.
4. Before pushing back on a leadership-endorsed direction, become the domain expert first — Vanessa Lee's API-versioning reversal came after a year of hands-on evidence, not a hunch, and that groundwork is what made a 45-minute conversation enough to reverse the CEO's stated position.
5. Lead with proof points, not opinion: concrete examples of the problem already happening in production carry more weight in a single conversation than any framework or slide deck.
6. Use the unfinished-artifact tactic deliberately: bring a rough, hand-drawn version of the proposed fix rather than a polished deck — a finished presentation signals "I've already done all the thinking," which leaves no room for the other person to contribute, while a visibly unfinished sketch invites pushback on the "how" while staying unshakeable on the "what's broken."
7. Separate what you're certain about from what's still open: be completely firm on the evidence that something is broken, but stay genuinely open on the design of the fix — this combination is what allows pushing hard without cornering the other person into a defensive stance.
8. Invite disagreement explicitly and repeatedly ("tell me where I'm wrong") rather than treating pushback as a threat to be managed — this is the difference between an ego-driven PM who treats being questioned as a threat to authority, and one who treats it as the mechanism that makes a team build something better than any individual could alone.
9. Connect any proposal to a real, high-stakes consequence (something breaks if we don't act, or something is won if we do) — proposals that don't connect to an "ugly human truth" compete with a thousand other priorities and rarely get real leadership attention.

## Inputs
- The specific update or document being prepared, and whether it currently traces back to a stated strategic pillar
- The evidence base for any pushback being considered — concrete, already-happened examples, not a hunch
- How much domain expertise has actually been built on the topic before attempting to push back on it
- The real stakes (what breaks, what's won) that the proposal connects to, if pushing for leadership attention

## Output format
A document restructured as an explicit hierarchy (vision/strategy/roadmap/PRD) with each item traceable to the pillar above it; for pushback specifically, a stated proof-point list (concrete, already-observed examples) paired with a deliberately rough sketch of the proposed fix, not a polished deck; and an explicit statement of what's non-negotiable (the evidence) versus what's genuinely open (the design).

## Example
A PM has spent a year noticing teams break an internal API in production and wants to convince a skeptical VP to version it — a structural change the VP has previously resisted. Rather than a slide deck, the PM brings four concrete, already-happened examples of teams breaking the API, plus a hand-drawn diagram of a quarterly versioning scheme sketched that morning. The unfinished sketch invites the VP to challenge the "how" (should it be quarterly or biannual?) while the four proof points make the "something is broken" half of the case unshakeable — leading to a reversed decision in one meeting rather than a multi-week campaign.

## Common pitfalls
- Presenting a roadmap or PRD item with no visible link back to a strategic pillar, leaving leadership unable to tell whether the work actually matters or is just activity.
- Bringing a polished, fully-baked deck to a pushback conversation, which signals "the thinking is already done" and leaves the other person nothing to contribute except acceptance or rejection.
- Treating disagreement from the team as a threat to be managed rather than actively inviting it — the PMs who struggle are consistently the ones optimizing for looking decisive instead of being right.
