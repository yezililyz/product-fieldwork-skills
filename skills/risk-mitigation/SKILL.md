# Risk & Mitigation

A standalone strategy framework — not sourced from a specific yezi-hub guide. Unlike the other skills in this library, this one isn't grounded in a real Product Fieldwork guide; it's included as a general-purpose consulting methodology worth having on hand.

## When to use
Use this when a strategy or major initiative is heading into governance review and the only risk documentation that exists is a generic, boilerplate list ("execution risk," "market risk," "talent risk") that could be copy-pasted onto any plan in the company and would tell a board nothing about this specific plan's actual exposure. It's also the right tool immediately after a strategy has been finalized, before implementation begins, since risks are cheapest to plan for before commitments are locked in. The signal to use it now: a board or steering committee asks "what could make this go wrong and who's watching for it," and the honest answer is a vague list with no owners and no early-warning mechanism. Without a scoped, specific risk register, organizations discover risks only after they've materialized, at which point mitigation options are far more limited and expensive.

## What it does
This skill builds a risk register scoped tightly to the specific strategy at hand — derived from its actual assumptions and dependencies rather than generic categories — scores and ranks each risk, and assigns a named owner and a concrete mitigation or contingency to every risk that matters. It closes by defining leading indicators that would signal a risk is starting to materialize while there is still time to act.

## Method
1. Scope the risk register specifically to this strategy: derive candidate risks from the strategy's own stated assumptions, dependencies, and points of external reliance (suppliers, regulators, key customers, key personnel, technology dependencies) rather than pulling from a generic enterprise risk taxonomy. A risk that isn't actually tied to this plan's logic doesn't belong on this register.
2. Categorize each risk by type — strategic (the plan's core logic is wrong), execution (the plan is right but delivery fails), external (market, regulatory, competitive shocks), and financial (funding, currency, cost volatility) — to ensure the register isn't skewed toward only the type of risk the team is most comfortable discussing.
3. Score each risk on likelihood and impact using a consistent scale (for example, 1-5 on each), and avoid the common failure of scoring impact only in financial terms — include reputational, regulatory, and strategic-optionality impact where relevant.
4. Rank risks by the combined likelihood-impact score, and apply a materiality cutoff: focus real mitigation planning on the top 5-8 risks rather than diluting attention across a long tail of low-probability, low-impact items that don't merit board-level discussion.
5. For each top risk, assign one named, accountable owner — not a department, a specific person — responsible for monitoring it and executing the mitigation if triggered.
6. Define a specific mitigation (an action taken now to reduce likelihood or impact) and a separate contingency (the response plan if the risk materializes anyway) for every top risk; these are different things and conflating them leaves a gap when a risk that was only "mitigated" still occurs.
7. Define a leading indicator for each top risk — a specific, monitorable metric or signal that would move before the risk fully materializes, so it can be treated as an early warning rather than discovered after the fact.
8. Set an explicit review cadence and re-scoping trigger: risk registers go stale as a strategy executes, so specify when the register gets re-reviewed (quarterly, or immediately after a major plan change) rather than treating it as a one-time document.

## Inputs
- The strategy document, including its explicit and implicit assumptions and dependencies
- Any history of past risks that have materialized in similar initiatives at this company
- Named candidates for risk ownership across the relevant functions
- Access to operational or market data that could serve as a leading indicator source

## Output format
A risk register scoped to this strategy, categorized by type, with likelihood and impact scores and a combined rank; for each top-ranked risk, a named owner, a distinct mitigation and contingency, and a specific leading indicator; and a stated review cadence for keeping the register current.

## Example
A manufacturing expansion plan that depends on a single overseas supplier for a critical component surfaces "single-source supply disruption" as its highest-ranked risk (high impact, moderate likelihood given known geopolitical volatility in the supplier's region). The register assigns the head of procurement as owner, sets mitigation as qualifying a second supplier within two quarters, sets contingency as pre-negotiated expedited freight terms with an alternate regional supplier, and defines the leading indicator as the supplier's own quoted lead times — a 20% increase in quoted lead time triggers escalation before an actual disruption occurs.

## Common pitfalls
- Building a generic risk list that could apply to any strategy, rather than deriving risks specifically from this plan's own assumptions and dependencies.
- Assigning risk ownership to a department or committee instead of a named individual, which diffuses accountability when the risk actually starts to materialize.
- Defining a mitigation but no separate contingency, leaving no defined response if the mitigation fails to prevent the risk from occurring.
