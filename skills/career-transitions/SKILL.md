# Career Transitions

Grounded in "Breaking Into AI & Search PM — Ankita Mishra AMA" and "The Renaissance Playbook — Mentorship, Networking & Community Craft."

## When to use
Use this when someone is optimizing a job search around a title ("I need to be a Product Manager") rather than around what the role actually stretches them to learn — the framing most likely to leave them credentialed but not effective once they get the title. It's also the right tool when a transition stalls because the person has no real network to pull from — no one to ask for an intro, no mentor, nothing beyond cold applications. The signal to use it now: someone's plan is entirely "apply to more postings" with no plan for building proof of work or relationships alongside it.

## What it does
This skill reframes the transition target from a title to a growth trajectory, lays out the three credible paths into product management (internal transition, early-stage believer, structured program) with each path's real risk profile, and builds the concrete relationship infrastructure — the email blurb, a lightweight warm-network habit, mentorship-seeking — that makes any transition actually move. Where the transition targets an AI/search-specific role, it layers in the deterministic-vs-probabilistic mental shift and company-specific interview prep from the AI-PM playbook.

## Method
1. Reframe the goal from a title to a growth trajectory: chase the work and the team on a high-growth trajectory, since growth is what creates new problems worth solving — and, eventually, the title anyway. A title alone is a lagging indicator.
2. Pick the transition path deliberately and know its risk: internal transition (slower, but arrives with context and trust already built — main risk is being typecast in your current function), early believer (join an early-stage startup as its first PM hire — fastest to the title, but the experience may not transfer if the company doesn't work out), or structured program (an APM/RPM-style program — competitive, but gives mentorship and a peer network that travels well).
3. If the target role is AI or search-specific, internalize the deterministic-vs-probabilistic shift first: traditional products behave the same way every time; AI/search products return a distribution of answers shaped by context — this reframes what "product sense" even means in that role.
4. Build real proof of work before or during the search: follow people actually doing the target job, exhaust free courses before paying for anything, and build one small concrete project rather than collecting buzzwords.
5. Find one real mentor, expecting to ask several people before one says yes — a real mentor changes a transition's trajectory the same way it changes an athlete's.
6. Write the introduction request as a proper email blurb: a one-line intro of who you are, enough context for the reader to act, and one specific, named ask — never "pick your brain." Make it copy-paste-forwardable for whoever's connecting you.
7. Keep the network warm on an ongoing basis, not just when something is needed: ask specific answerable questions, follow up quickly after first contact, reconnect with small no-ask check-ins, and give before asking.
8. If negotiating an offer as part of the transition, build the three-list overlap: what you want (stack-ranked), what the company wants (from the JD and interviews), and where they overlap — anchor the ask on the overlap, not on your list alone, since early-stage companies can rarely flex on cash but often can flex on equity, scope, or timeline.

## Inputs
- The specific role or domain being transitioned into, and whether it's AI/search-specific
- Current proof of work, side projects, or transferable experience already in hand
- Existing network — any warm intros, prior colleagues, or communities to draw on
- Whether an internal transfer path exists at the person's current company

## Output format
A named growth-trajectory target (not just a title); a chosen transition path (internal/early-believer/structured-program) with its risk named explicitly; a concrete proof-of-work plan; a drafted introduction-request email in the three-part blurb format; and, if AI/search-specific, a company-tailored interview prep brief.

## Example
A data analyst wants to move into product management at an AI-forward company. The growth-trajectory reframe points them toward an internal transfer at their current company (which already has an AI initiative), rather than cold-applying externally — lower risk, and their domain trust already exists. They build one small AI-assisted prototype as concrete proof of work, draft a specific email blurb requesting a 20-minute conversation with their company's one AI PM (not "would love to pick your brain"), and prep for eventual interviews using the deterministic-vs-probabilistic framing rather than generic PM answers.

## Common pitfalls
- Optimizing the search around a specific title rather than a growth trajectory, landing a title without a role that actually stretches or teaches anything.
- Sending vague "would love to pick your brain" outreach instead of a specific, one-ask, forwardable email blurb — the version that's actually easy for someone to act on.
- Choosing the early-believer path (first PM hire at an unproven startup) without weighing that the experience may not transfer if the company doesn't work out — the fastest path to a title isn't always the lowest-risk path to a career.
