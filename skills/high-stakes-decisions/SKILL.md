# High-Stakes Decisions

Grounded in "Beyond Prioritization — The Field Guide," "Business Strategy — Creating Competitive Advantage," and "Problem-Solving & Framing Toolkit for Product Management," plus a standalone situation-assessment framework (not sourced from a specific yezi-hub guide) for establishing a fact-based baseline before any diagnosis begins.

## When to use
Use this at the very start of any high-stakes engagement, before any direction is proposed, when leadership has a vague sense that something needs attention but the actual diagnosis hasn't been done — the org is debating solutions before agreeing on the problem, or two executives describe "what's going on" in materially different terms that neither has checked against the underlying data. It's equally the right tool once a decision is being made on whatever data happened to be handy, rather than on what would actually have to be true for the decision to change — the consulting insight that starting from data produces pattern-hunting and false confidence, while starting from a testable hypothesis keeps analysis purposeful. It's also the right tool when a "problem" being solved is really a symptom (sales are down, churn is high) restated as if it were a cause — fixing a symptom only suppresses it temporarily, and it will recur. The signal to use it now: a proposed fix would leave the underlying mechanism completely unchanged if implemented, or previous attempts to fix the same issue haven't worked, which is often a sign the team solved for a plausible-sounding cause rather than the one the facts actually support.

## What it does
This skill first establishes a rigorous, fact-based picture of current performance across financial, market, and operational lenses, structured into an issue hierarchy that separates confirmed fact from inference and open unknowns and anchored to the one decision question leadership actually needs answered. From that baseline, it separates symptom from root cause using the 5 Whys and fishbone diagrams, decomposes the problem space with MECE structure so nothing overlaps or gets missed, and replaces open-ended exploration with hypothesis-driven analysis — stating what must be true for the decision to change, then testing only that, prioritized by impact and uncertainty. For decisions with real business-strategy stakes, it layers in competitive-advantage analysis (VRIN, Five Forces) to check that a "great" option is also defensible, not just correct.

## Method
1. Frame the engagement using a situation-complication-question structure: state the shared situation everyone agrees on, name the complication that has changed and made the status quo untenable, and state precisely the one decision question this work needs to answer — anchoring to a single question up front prevents the assessment from sprawling into an unfocused general review.
2. Triangulate current performance across three independent lenses rather than relying on any single one: financial (revenue growth, margin trend, cash position), market (share trend, demand signals, competitive positioning), and operational (throughput, quality, cost-to-serve) — gathered at a granularity fine enough to spot where a problem is concentrated (segment, region, product line, cohort), since a problem that looks purely financial often has its root cause visible only in the operational or market lens.
3. Build a MECE issue tree from the top-level symptom down through contributing factors to candidate root causes, and mark every branch explicitly as fact (directly verified by data), inference (a reasoned conclusion from indirect data), or unknown (plausible, no evidence either way) — this discipline is what prevents assumptions from being quietly treated as established findings later on. Plot the headline findings on an impact-vs-certainty 2x2 to identify the load-bearing issues versus what merely needs more investigation versus what's noise.
4. Distinguish symptom from root cause with a three-part test on the load-bearing issues from the baseline: does the statement describe an outcome or a mechanism? Can the team actually control it? If fixed, would the same problem almost certainly not recur? A "no" on any of these means you're still at the symptom level.
5. Run the 5 Whys to drill from symptom to mechanism, anchoring each "why" in observable facts rather than opinion, and stopping at a specific, actionable design element (a policy, process, or capability) rather than drifting into unfixable territory like "culture" or "the market." For genuinely multi-variable problems, use a fishbone diagram (people, process, technology, materials, environment, measurement) to surface candidate causes across categories without narrowing prematurely — each branch is a hypothesis to test, not a verdict.
6. Convert the sharpest branches into explicit, falsifying hypotheses in the format "if [X], then [Y], because [Z]" — checking each for four qualities: specific, verifiable, problem-relevant, and not itself another symptom in disguise.
7. Prioritize which hypotheses to test first on impact × uncertainty: high impact and high uncertainty gets tested first (the biggest unknowns); low impact and low uncertainty gets deprioritized or just monitored. Design the minimum analysis needed to decisively support or reject each — stop once a hypothesis is decisively resolved unless further precision would actually change the recommendation, and run a sensitivity check to quantify how much the conclusion could shift.
8. For strategically consequential decisions (not just operational ones), check the surviving option against durability, not just correctness: does it pass VRIN (valuable, rare, inimitable, non-substitutable) as a real competitive advantage, or is it merely good and easily matched? A correct decision that's easy for a competitor to copy is a weaker bet than a slightly less obvious one that's actually defensible.
9. Close by naming the specific open questions and next analyses that would resolve the highest-impact remaining unknowns, and translate the final recommendation into the decision-maker's own language and stakes — dollar impact, risk reduction, or decision speed — rather than leaving it as an analytical artifact nobody can act on.

## Inputs
- Recent financial performance data (revenue, margin, cash) at a segment or product-line level of granularity, plus market and operational metrics relevant to the business
- The specific question or concern leadership has raised that triggered the engagement
- Access to whoever holds tacit knowledge of the mechanism (the people closest to where the problem actually occurs)
- Enough data access to test the 2-3 highest-priority hypotheses specifically, not a full data dump
- The decision-maker's own stakes and language, to translate the eventual recommendation

## Output format
A one-page fact base organized by the three lenses with a stated momentum read (improving/stable/deteriorating, and where); a MECE issue tree with every branch tagged fact/inference/unknown and plotted on an impact-vs-certainty 2x2; a root-cause statement that passes the three-part symptom test; a small set of hypotheses in "if/then/because" format, each rated on impact and uncertainty; the specific analysis run against the top 1-2 hypotheses with a clear supported/rejected verdict; a VRIN check on the surviving option's actual defensibility for strategic decisions; and a list of remaining open questions with the next analysis needed to close each one.

## Example
Leadership asks why enterprise renewal rates are declining and initially suspects pricing. The triangulated fact base shows financial data confirms the decline is real and accelerating, but the operational lens shows it's heavily concentrated in accounts onboarded through a specific channel partner in the last two quarters — not spread evenly across the enterprise base as a pricing problem would predict; the issue tree marks "pricing is the cause" as an unconfirmed inference and "the decline is concentrated in one onboarding cohort" as a verified fact. The three-part symptom test confirms "churn is up" is still an outcome, not a mechanism. Five Whys traces the confirmed cohort finding to a specific, controllable cause: no structured onboarding follow-up exists past day 3 for that partner's accounts. The hypothesis "if we add a day-7 and day-14 check-in for this cohort, then 90-day retention improves, because early unaddressed confusion is currently driving silent churn" is tested on a contained sample before being rolled out broadly — resolving the ambiguity before committing the whole team to a fix, and before any pricing action is considered at all.

## Common pitfalls
- Starting to debate solutions before the situation baseline is complete, which locks in a diagnosis that hasn't actually been checked against the data.
- Treating every finding as equally weighted instead of separating fact from inference from unknown, which lets an unverified assumption quietly become the basis for the whole strategy — or treating a symptom (sales are down, NPS is low) as if it were already the root cause, guaranteeing recurrence.
- Analyzing all available data before forming any hypothesis, producing pattern-hunting and false confidence instead of a decision-ready answer — hypothesis-first framing exists specifically to prevent this.
- Picking the "correct" strategic option without checking whether it's actually defensible (VRIN) — a good idea a competitor can trivially copy is a much weaker high-stakes bet than it first appears.
