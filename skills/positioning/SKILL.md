# Positioning

Grounded in "Product Strategy — A Framework for Filtering Great from Good" and "Business Strategy — Creating Competitive Advantage."

## When to use
Use this when a positioning statement claims a differentiator that any competitor could just as easily claim — sounding compelling in a deck without passing the VRIN test (valuable, rare, inimitable, non-substitutable) that actually determines whether it's a real, defensible advantage. It's also the right tool when repositioning after a competitive shift, since the moment worth studying is exactly when a factor that used to be an advantage (Netflix's early streaming-tech edge) becomes table stakes once competitors catch up — the defensible moat has to move, and positioning has to move with it.

## What it does
This skill tests a candidate positioning claim against two independent filters: the principles filter (does it pass differentiated, time-sensitive, and frequently used — or is it merely a "nice to have" dressed up as a differentiator) and VRIN (is the underlying capability actually rare and hard to copy, or just currently unmatched by coincidence). It also applies the generic-strategies framing (cost leadership, differentiation, focus) to make sure the positioning isn't accidentally "stuck in the middle" — trying to win on both price and uniqueness at once.

## Method
1. State the positioning candidate as a specific claim, not a vibe — what does this product do that a named alternative doesn't, and why would a customer care right now.
2. Run the principles filter on the claim: is it differentiated (sets the product apart, not something any competitor could claim), time-sensitive (needed in the moment, not just a convenience), and frequently used (something customers return to often, not a one-off nicety)? A claim failing any of the three is a "nice to have," not real positioning.
3. Run VRIN on the underlying capability behind the claim: is it Valuable (exploits a real opportunity), Rare (few competitors have it), Inimitable (genuinely hard to copy, not just uncopied so far), and Non-substitutable (can't be swapped for a generic alternative)? A capability that's valuable and rare but easily imitated is a temporary advantage, not a defensible position.
4. Watch for advantages that were real once and have since become table stakes — the moment a formerly-rare capability becomes commonly available (streaming tech, in Netflix's case), the positioning built on it has to move to whatever capability is now the actually-defensible one (originals content, subscriber data).
5. Check the positioning against the generic-strategies framework: is the product clearly choosing cost leadership (win on operational efficiency and price), differentiation (win on genuine uniqueness worth a premium), or focus (win by serving one segment better than generalists can)? A positioning statement that tries to claim both cheap and unique simultaneously is "stuck in the middle" — the classic failure mode.
6. Trace the positioning down into the value chain: for each activity (operations, marketing, tech development), is the company investing to differentiate or deliberately using a cheaper/partnered approach elsewhere? The Starbucks-in-China pattern shows the discipline: invest where it differentiates (in-store experience), partner or use cheaper options where it doesn't (delivery logistics via a partner platform).
7. State the one-page canvas version for stakeholder alignment: a one-line vision, the named customer pain points being solved, and which pillar (Foundation — making the core solid, or Growth — expanding reach into a new solution/vertical/role) this positioning claim primarily serves.
8. Revisit the positioning whenever a competitor closes the gap on the claimed differentiator — a positioning statement isn't a one-time artifact; it needs the same VRIN check re-run periodically as the competitive landscape shifts.

## Inputs
- The candidate positioning statement or claim, stated specifically against a named alternative
- The actual underlying capability the claim rests on, to run the VRIN test against
- Where the product currently sits on cost-leadership vs. differentiation vs. focus, to check for a "stuck in the middle" problem
- Any recent competitive moves that might have closed the gap on the claimed differentiator

## Output format
A stated positioning claim with a VRIN verdict on its underlying capability (core competency vs. merely useful and copyable); a principles-filter check (differentiated / time-sensitive / frequently-used, pass/fail on each); a stated generic-strategy choice (cost leadership / differentiation / focus) with a check against "stuck in the middle"; and, where relevant, a note on which capability the positioning should shift to if the currently-claimed one has become table stakes.

## Example
A company positions itself as "the fastest option in the category." VRIN testing reveals speed is valuable and currently rare, but not genuinely inimitable — a well-funded competitor could match it within a release cycle. The real, harder-to-copy asset turns out to be proprietary usage data feeding a recommendation engine competitors can't replicate without years of the same data. The positioning shifts from "fastest" (a claim with a closing window) to a claim built on the recommendation quality that data actually enables — a slower-forming but much more defensible position.

## Common pitfalls
- Positioning around a capability that's currently unmatched but not genuinely inimitable — a claim that sounds defensible until a well-resourced competitor closes the gap in one release cycle.
- Trying to claim both lowest cost and genuine differentiation in the same positioning statement, landing "stuck in the middle" — the classic failure mode neither cost-focused nor differentiation-focused customers find compelling.
- Failing to notice when a once-real differentiator has become table stakes across the category, and continuing to lead with it in messaging long after competitors caught up.
