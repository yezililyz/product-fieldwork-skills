# Defining Product Strategy

Grounded in "Product Strategy — A Framework for Filtering Great from Good."

## When to use
Use this when a "strategy doc" is really just a prioritized feature list with no principle underneath explaining why those features and not others. It's also the right tool when a team can't say no to a stakeholder's request because there's no stated filter to say no with — a principle that never rejects an idea isn't actually a principle. The signal to use it now: two people read the same strategy doc and walk away with different ideas about what the team should build next quarter.

## What it does
This skill builds strategy top-down through five layers of increasing concreteness (vision → principles → mission → strategic plays → tactics → metrics), each with a feedback loop back up so learning updates the strategy rather than requiring a rewrite. It then applies two filtering tools — principles that test ideas for being differentiated, time-sensitive, and frequently used, and the MAPA pillar test (Measurable, Achievable, Prioritized, Aligned) — to separate a real strategic bet from an aspiration that merely sounds strategic.

## Method
1. Build the pyramid top-down: a fixed-destination Vision (the value proposition, the North Star), Principles (core values that filter every idea below), Mission (the route from here to the vision), Strategic Plays (the handful of big bets), Tactics (the roadmap activities executing each play), and Metrics (proof across customer, business, tech, and partner).
2. Collapse the top of the pyramid onto a one-page canvas for stakeholder socialization: a one-line vision, the named customer pain points, a Foundation pillar (core functionality, UX, extensibility) and a Growth pillar (new solutions, verticals, or roles) — sequence Foundation bets so Growth bets land on solid ground.
3. Apply the principles filter to every candidate idea: does it pass all three tests — differentiated (sets you apart, not something any competitor could claim), time-sensitive (needed in the moment, not just convenient), and frequently used (people return to it often)? An idea failing even one test gets set aside, however good it sounds.
4. Insist principles reject the team's own favorite ideas, not just other people's requests — a principle that only filters out other stakeholders' asks isn't doing its job.
5. Before a strategic pillar earns roadmap space, run the MAPA test: is it Measurable (tied to a metric you can actually move), Achievable (realistic given team/tech/timeline), Prioritized (ranked against everything else, not just "important" in isolation), and Aligned (agreed with execs, not held solo)? A pillar failing any test is an aspiration, not a strategy.
6. When looking for strategy candidates, use the Why-Stack: start from a symptom, keep asking "why" until you hit a root cause, then treat each layer of the stack as a candidate strategic direction worth filtering through the principles and MAPA tests above.
7. Score surviving candidates on the prioritization grid: Customer + Business + Differentiation, minus Technology cost — the first three should be high, technology cost should be low; a high tech-cost item isn't disqualified, but it needs to earn that cost with proportionally higher scores elsewhere.
8. Communicate the finished strategy at the altitude leadership actually needs: lead with what matters (inverted pyramid), communicate strategy and the reasoning behind bets — not execution-level ticket detail — and name risks/pivots early so nothing lands as a surprise.

## Inputs
- The current draft strategy or roadmap, if one exists, to check for a real underlying filter
- Candidate strategic ideas or pillars to run through the principles and MAPA tests
- The stated (or absent) vision and customer pain points this strategy is meant to serve
- Any symptom or recurring complaint to Why-Stack down to a root-cause strategy candidate

## Output format
A completed top-down pyramid (vision through metrics) or, at minimum, the one-page canvas (vision, problem, Foundation pillar, Growth pillar); a principles-filter verdict (pass/fail on differentiated/time-sensitive/frequently-used) for each candidate idea under consideration; a MAPA verdict for each pillar being considered for the roadmap; and, where relevant, a scored prioritization grid ranking the surviving candidates.

## Example
A team drafts a pillar: "improve engagement." Run through MAPA, it fails immediately — not Measurable (no defined metric), not Prioritized (competing with nothing specific). Why-Stacking the underlying symptom ("casual users rarely return") down through five whys surfaces two sharper candidates: improve content recommendations, and make existing communities more visible. Both pass the principles filter (differentiated from the generic feed, time-sensitive at the return-visit moment, frequently triggered); scored on the prioritization grid, the community-visibility pillar wins on lower tech cost for comparable customer and business impact, and becomes the stated strategic play — with a real metric attached.

## Common pitfalls
- Writing a "strategy" that's actually a longer, better-organized feature wishlist, with no principle that has ever said no to an idea.
- Treating a vague aspiration ("improve engagement," "delight users") as a strategic pillar without running it through the MAPA test, where it fails on Measurable and Prioritized immediately.
- Communicating strategy at the execution altitude (ticket-level detail) instead of the why-and-bets altitude leadership actually needs, burying the one page that matters inside a roadmap deck nobody reads in full.
